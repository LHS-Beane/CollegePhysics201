Physical Setup

A uniform solid disk of mass $M = 4.0\\,\text{kg}$ and radius $R = 0.40\\,\text{m}$ can rotate freely about a horizontal axle through its center. A light, non-stretching string is wrapped around the rim and attached to a hanging block of mass $m = 1.2\\,\text{kg}$, as shown. The system is released from rest and the block falls vertically while the disk spins up.

The axle is frictionless, and the string does not slip on the disk. You may use $g = 9.8\\,\text{m/s}^2$.

  • The moment of inertia of a solid disk about its center is $I = \\tfrac{1}{2}MR^2$.
  • There is no air resistance, and the string remains taut.

Later modification:

After the block has fallen some distance and the disk is spinning, the string is cut and the disk continues to spin freely with angular speed $\\omega_0$. At that instant, a small blob of putty of mass $m_p = 0.50\\,\\text{kg}$ is dropped onto the rim and sticks to the disk. The putty lands tangentially (so its initial speed relative to the ground is the same as the rim speed at that point).

Part (a) – Angular Acceleration of the Disk

While the block is still moving and the string remains taut, the block has linear acceleration $a$ downward, and the disk has angular acceleration $\\alpha$.

Using Newton’s 2nd law for the block and the torque equation for the disk, find the magnitude of the angular acceleration $\\alpha$ of the disk in rad/s$^2$.

rad/s²

For the block: $mg - T = ma$. For the disk: the torque from the string is $\\tau = TR$, and $\\tau = I\\alpha$ with $I = \\tfrac12 MR^2$.

Use the no-slip condition $a = \\alpha R$ to eliminate $a$ or $\\alpha$ and solve for $\\alpha$.

Show solution for part (a)

Step 1: Force equation for the block (taking down as positive):

\\[ mg - T = ma. \\]

Step 2: Torque equation for the disk:

The tension $T$ exerts a torque $\\tau = TR$ about the center. For a solid disk, $I = \\tfrac12 MR^2$, so \\[ TR = I\\alpha = \\tfrac12 MR^2 \\alpha. \\] Using $a = \\alpha R$, we can write \\[ T = \\frac{I\\alpha}{R} = \\frac{I}{R^2} a = \\tfrac12 M a. \\]

Step 3: Substitute $T = \\tfrac12 Ma$ into the block equation:

\\[ mg - \\tfrac12 M a = m a \\quad\\Rightarrow\\quad mg = a\\left(m + \\tfrac12 M\\right). \\]

Solve for $a$: \\[ a = \\frac{mg}{m + M/2} = \\frac{(1.2)(9.8)}{1.2 + 4.0/2} \\approx 3.68\\,\\text{m/s}^2. \\]

Step 4: Use $a = \\alpha R$ to find $\\alpha$:

\\[ \\alpha = \\frac{a}{R} = \\frac{3.68}{0.40} \\approx 9.19\\,\\text{rad/s}^2. \\]

Answer: $\\alpha \\approx 9.2\\,\\text{rad/s}^2$.

Part (b) – Tension in the String

Still considering the motion while the block is accelerating downward and the disk is spinning up, find the tension $T$ in the string.

N

From part (a), you should have found $a$. The block equation $mg - T = ma$ can now be solved directly for $T$.

Show solution for part (b)

From part (a), $a \\approx 3.68\\,\\text{m/s}^2$. Use the block’s force equation:

\\[ mg - T = ma \\quad\\Rightarrow\\quad T = mg - ma = m(g - a). \\]

Plug in numbers: \\[ T = (1.2\\,\\text{kg})\\big(9.8 - 3.68\\big)\\,\\text{m/s}^2 \\approx 7.35\\,\\text{N}. \\]

Answer: $T \\approx 7.35\\,\\text{N}$.

Part (c) – Speed After Falling 1.5 m

The block is released from rest and falls a distance of $h = 1.5\\,\\text{m}$ before you examine the motion. Assume the acceleration remains constant (the string is still attached and taut).

Find the speed of the block after it has fallen $1.5\\,\\text{m}$. Then find the corresponding angular speed $\\omega_0$ of the disk at that instant.

m/s

With constant acceleration, you can use either kinematics $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\\Delta y$ (with $v_0 = 0$), or an energy argument.

Once you know $v$, use the no-slip condition $v = \\omega_0 R$ to get $\\omega_0$.

rad/s

Show solution for part (c)

Step 1: Block speed from constant acceleration.

The block starts from rest, so $v_0 = 0$, and $a \\approx 3.68\\,\\text{m/s}^2$ (from part (a)). Use \\[ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a h = 0 + 2(3.68)(1.5). \\]

\\[ v = \\sqrt{2(3.68)(1.5)} \\approx 3.32\\,\\text{m/s}. \\]

Step 2: Relate $v$ and $\\omega_0$ at the rim.

No slipping on the rim means \\[ v = \\omega_0 R \\quad\\Rightarrow\\quad \\omega_0 = \\frac{v}{R}. \\]

\\[ \\omega_0 = \\frac{3.32}{0.40} \\approx 8.30\\,\\text{rad/s}. \\]

Answers: $v \\approx 3.32\\,\\text{m/s}$, $\\omega_0 \\approx 8.30\\,\\text{rad/s}$.

Part (d) – Inelastic Collision at the Rim (Angular Momentum)

After the block has fallen $1.5\\,\\text{m}$, the string is cut. The block is removed and we now focus only on the spinning disk. At that instant, the disk has angular speed $\\omega_0$ (from part (c)).

A blob of putty of mass $m_p = 0.50\\,\\text{kg}$ drops vertically onto the rim and sticks to the disk at radius $R$. Just before it lands, the putty has negligible horizontal speed, so its angular momentum about the disk’s center is approximately zero.

Find the new angular speed $\\omega_f$ of the disk–putty system immediately after the putty sticks.

rad/s

Treat this as an inelastic “sticking” collision in rotation. External torque about the rotation axis is negligible during the brief collision, so angular momentum is conserved.

Before: only the disk has angular momentum ($I_{\\text{disk}} \\omega_0$). After: disk + point mass at radius $R$ have moment of inertia $I_{\\text{disk}} + m_p R^2$.

Show solution for part (d)

Step 1: Initial and final angular momentum.

The putty has negligible angular momentum about the center just before sticking, so the initial angular momentum is \\[ L_i = I_{\\text{disk}} \\omega_0. \\] For a solid disk, \\[ I_{\\text{disk}} = \\tfrac12 MR^2 = \\tfrac12 (4.0)(0.40)^2 = 0.32\\,\\text{kg·m}^2. \\]

After the collision, the putty is stuck at radius $R$, so its moment of inertia is $m_p R^2$. The total moment of inertia is \\[ I_f = I_{\\text{disk}} + m_p R^2 = 0.32 + (0.50)(0.40)^2 = 0.32 + 0.08 = 0.40\\,\\text{kg·m}^2. \\]

Step 2: Angular momentum conservation.

\\[ L_i = L_f \\quad\\Rightarrow\\quad I_{\\text{disk}}\\omega_0 = I_f \\omega_f. \\]

Solve for $\\omega_f$: \\[ \\omega_f = \\frac{I_{\\text{disk}}}{I_f} \\omega_0 = \\frac{0.32}{0.40} \\omega_0 = 0.80\\,\\omega_0. \\]

Using $\\omega_0 \\approx 8.30\\,\\text{rad/s}$ (from part (c)): \\[ \\omega_f \\approx 0.80(8.30) \\approx 6.64\\,\\text{rad/s}. \\]

Answer: $\\omega_f \\approx 6.64\\,\\text{rad/s}$.

Part (e) – Energy Loss in the Inelastic Collision

Compare the rotational kinetic energy of the disk–putty system before and after the putty sticks. What fraction (in percent) of the rotational kinetic energy is lost in the collision?

%

Rotational kinetic energy is $K = \\tfrac12 I\\omega^2$. Compute $K_i$ using $I_{\\text{disk}}$ and $\\omega_0$, and $K_f$ using $I_f = I_{\\text{disk}} + m_p R^2$ and $\\omega_f$ from part (d).

Then the percentage loss is \\[ \\%\\text{ loss} = \\frac{K_i - K_f}{K_i} \\times 100\\%. \\]

Show solution for part (e)

Step 1: Initial rotational energy (disk only).

\\[ K_i = \\tfrac12 I_{\\text{disk}} \\omega_0^2 = \\tfrac12 (0.32) \\omega_0^2. \\]

Step 2: Final rotational energy (disk + putty).

\\[ K_f = \\tfrac12 I_f \\omega_f^2 = \\tfrac12 (0.40) \\omega_f^2. \\]

From part (d), $\\omega_f = 0.80\\,\\omega_0$. Substitute: \\[ K_f = \\tfrac12 (0.40) (0.80^2 \\omega_0^2) = 0.20 \\cdot 0.64\\,\\omega_0^2 = 0.128\\,\\omega_0^2. \\]

For the initial energy: \\[ K_i = \\tfrac12 (0.32) \\omega_0^2 = 0.16\\,\\omega_0^2. \\]

Step 3: Fraction and percentage of energy lost.

\\[ \\frac{K_f}{K_i} = \\frac{0.128\\,\\omega_0^2}{0.16\\,\\omega_0^2} = 0.80. \\]

So $K_f$ is $80\\%$ of $K_i$, meaning $20\\%$ of the initial rotational kinetic energy is lost in the inelastic sticking collision: \\[ \\%\\text{ loss} = 100\\% - 80\\% = 20\\%. \\]

Answer: about $20\\%$ of the rotational kinetic energy is lost.
