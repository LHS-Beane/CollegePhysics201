    üîµ Rotational Kinematics

    Rotational Kinematics

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    Rotational motion uses three primary angular variables:

    \[ \theta,\qquad \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt},\qquad \alpha = \frac{d\omega}{dt} \]
    • Œ∏ ‚Äî angular position (radians)
    • œâ ‚Äî angular velocity
    • Œ± ‚Äî angular acceleration

    Radians are dimensionless, making calculus and physical relations clean.

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    Hewitt notes that rotational motion is simply "motion that turns about an axis."

    As an object rotates through an angle Œ∏, a point at radius r moves through arc length:

    \[ s = r\theta \]

    This leads directly to the linear ‚Üî rotational relationships:

    \[ v = r\omega $$ $$ a_t = r\alpha $$ $$ a_c = \omega^2 r \]
    • Tangential acceleration changes speed.
    • Centripetal acceleration changes direction.

    üéì Advanced Concept

    Rotational kinematics parallels linear kinematics exactly:

    Linear Rotational
    \(x\) \(\theta\)
    \(v=\frac{dx}{dt}\) \(\omega=\frac{d\theta}{dt}\)
    \(a=\frac{dv}{dt}\) \(\alpha=\frac{d\omega}{dt}\)

    This analogy is the foundation of rotational dynamics and energy.

    üî∂ Worked Example: Finding Angular Velocity

    Worked Example: Constant Angular Acceleration

    A wheel starts from rest and accelerates at \( \alpha = 3 \text{ rad/s}^2 \).
    Find \( \omega \) after 4 seconds.

    Step 1 ‚Äî Use definition
    $$ \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t $$

    Step 2 ‚Äî Plug values
    $$ \omega = 0 + (3)(4) = 12 \text{ rad/s} $$

    Answer:
    \(\omega = 12 \text{ rad/s}\)

    üîµ Torque

    Torque

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    Torque measures how effectively a force causes rotation:

    \[ \tau = rF\sin\theta \]

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    Torque depends on: - force magnitude
    - lever arm length
    - angle of application

    Hewitt: "Only the perpendicular part of the force twists the object."

    Direction is given by the right-hand rule:

    üéì Advanced Concept

    Key definitions: - Lever arm (r): distance from pivot to force line
    - Effective force: \(F_\perp = F\sin\theta\)
    - Net torque: sum of all clockwise (‚àí) and counterclockwise (+) torques

    üî∂ Worked Example: Torque at an Angle

    Worked Example: Single Torque

    A 0.40 m bar experiences a 15 N force at \(60^\circ\).

    Compute torque: $$ \tau = rF\sin\theta = (0.40)(15)\sin 60^\circ $$

    \[ \tau = 6 \times 0.866 = 5.20 \text{ N¬∑m} \]

    Answer:
    \( \tau = 5.2\text{ N¬∑m} \)

    üî∂ Worked Example: Competing Torques

    Worked Example: Net Torque

    A bar has two forces: - 20 N at 0.30 m (CCW) - 15 N at 0.50 m (CW)

    \[ \tau_{CCW} = (0.30)(20) = 6.0 $$ $$ \tau_{CW} = (0.50)(15) = 7.5 \]
    \[ \tau_{net} = 6.0 - 7.5 = -1.5 \text{ N¬∑m} \]

    Clockwise rotation.

    üîµ Moment of Inertia

    Moment of Inertia

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    Moment of inertia is rotational mass:

    \[ I = \sum m r^2 \]

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    Knight emphasizes:
    "An object's resistance to rotation depends WHERE its mass is located."

    Object Moment of Inertia
    Solid disk \( \frac12 MR^2 \)
    Hoop \( MR^2 \)
    Rod (center) \( \frac{1}{12} ML^2 \)
    Rod (end) \( \frac{1}{3} ML^2 \)

    üéì Advanced Concept

    Parallel-Axis Theorem:

    \[ I = I_{cm} + Md^2 \]

    Used when rotating about any axis NOT through the center.

    üî∂ Worked Example: Using Parallel-Axis

    Worked Example: Rod About One End

    A rod of mass 2.0 kg and length 1.0 m rotates about one end.

    Center moment: $$ I_{cm} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 = \frac{1}{12}(2)(1)^2 = 0.167 $$

    Shift to end: $$ I = I_{cm} + Md^2 = 0.167 + 2(0.5)^2 $$

    \[ I = 0.167 + 0.5 = 0.667 \text{ kg¬∑m}^2 \]

    üîµ Rotational Dynamics (œÑ = IŒ±)

    Rotational Dynamics

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    \[ \tau_{net} = I\alpha \]

    The rotational form of Newton‚Äôs 2nd Law.

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    • Larger torque ‚Üí larger Œ±
    • Larger inertia ‚Üí smaller Œ±
    • Direction of Œ± determined by direction of œÑ

    üéì Advanced Concept

    Pulley systems combine: - translation of masses
    - rotation of pulleys

    üî∂ Worked Example: Rotating Disk

    Worked Example: Tangential Force

    Disk: \(m=2.0\text{ kg}, r=0.20\text{ m}\)
    Tangential force: \(10\text{ N}\)

    Torque: $$ \tau = rF = 2.0 \text{ N¬∑m} $$

    Inertia: $$ I = \frac12 mr^2 = 0.04 $$

    Acceleration: $$ \alpha = \frac{\tau}{I} = 50 $$

    üîµ Rolling Without Slipping

    Rolling Without Slipping

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    Condition:

    \[ v = r\omega \]

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    Hewitt:
    "Rolling is walking forward while spinning around."

    The bottom point is instantaneously at rest.

    Static friction provides the link ‚Äî but does no work.

    üéì Advanced Concept

    • Bottom moves at 0
    • Center moves at v
    • Top moves at 2v

    üîµ Rotational Energy

    Rotational Energy

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    Total kinetic energy:

    \[ K = \frac12 mv^2 + \frac12 I\omega^2 \]

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    A hoop accelerates slower than a disk.
    A sphere accelerates faster than both.

    Because I determines how KE is split.

    üéì Advanced Concept

    üîµ Angular Momentum

    Angular Momentum

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    \[ L = I\omega \]

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    Hewitt:
    "Angular momentum is the rotational 'oomph' of an object."

    It depends on: - how fast it‚Äôs spinning
    - how mass is distributed

    üéì Advanced Concept

    Vector form: $$ \vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p} $$

    Direction: right-hand rule.

    üîµ Conservation of Angular Momentum

    Conservation of Angular Momentum

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    \[ L_i = L_f $$ $$ I_i \omega_i = I_f \omega_f \]

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    Figure skaters: bring arms in ‚Üí spin faster.

    Stars collapse ‚Üí rotate rapidly.

    üéì Advanced Concept

    • No external torque ‚Üí angular momentum fixed
    • Internal changes (like shifting mass) affect œâ

    üîµ Gyroscopes & Precession

    Gyroscopes & Precession

    ‚≠ê Core Idea

    Precession occurs when torque acts perpendicular to angular momentum.

    üåü Deeper Reasoning

    Hewitt:
    ‚ÄúA gyroscope doesn‚Äôt fall ‚Äî it changes direction.‚Äù

    Because: $$ \vec{\tau} = \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt} $$

    üéì Advanced Concept

    Applications: - Bicycles
    - Rockets
    - Navigation systems
    - Fidget spinners
