    1. Physical Quantities, Units, and Measurement

    Physics describes the world using measurable quantities: - length, time, mass, temperature, etc. - each must have a unit (meters, seconds, kilograms, kelvins‚Ä¶)

    We use the SI (International System of Units) as a common language.

    Quantity Unit Symbol
    Length meter m
    Mass kilogram kg
    Time second s
    Temperature kelvin K
    Electric current ampere A

    Visual from your slides:

    A ‚Äúnumber‚Äù without a unit is meaningless in physics.

    Compare: - ‚ÄúThe car went 30‚Äù ‚Üí 30 what? - ‚ÄúThe car went 30 m‚Äù vs ‚Äú30 mi‚Äù vs ‚Äú30 km‚Äù ‚Üí very different physical situations.

    Units also hide physical scales:
    - meters vs nanometers vs light-years
    - seconds vs microseconds vs years

    Your slide likely showing different scales:

    This helps students see that the same laws apply from atoms to galaxies.

    When you later do more advanced physics (EM, quantum, thermo), being sloppy with units leads to: - wrong answers - confusion about what an equation actually means - difficulty catching mistakes

    Habit to build now: - Always write the unit next to your answer. - Always check if unit types ‚Äúmatch‚Äù on both sides of equations.

    2. SI Prefixes and Unit Conversions (Factor‚ÄìLabel Method)

    We use prefixes to handle very large or small numbers:

    Prefix Symbol Factor
    kilo k \(10^3\)
    mega M \(10^6\)
    giga G \(10^9\)
    milli m \(10^{-3}\)
    micro Œº \(10^{-6}\)
    nano n \(10^{-9}\)

    From your slides:

    We do conversions using the factor‚Äìlabel method (multiplying by 1 cleverly).

    Example: Convert 60 mph to m/s

    \[ 60\ \frac{\text{mi}}{\text{hr}} \cdot \frac{1609\ \text{m}}{1\ \text{mi}} \cdot \frac{1\ \text{hr}}{3600\ \text{s}} = 26.8\ \text{m/s} \]

    Each ‚Äúconversion factor‚Äù is just a fancy version of:

    \[ \frac{\text{same quantity in different units}}{\text{same quantity in original units}} = 1 \]

    So we are multiplying by 1, but in a way that changes units.

    In more advanced problems, you might convert: - km/h to m/s
    - g/cm\(^3\) to kg/m\(^3\)
    - J to eV (electron-volts)

    Practice problem:

    Convert \( 3.5\ \text{g/cm}^3 \) into kg/m\(^3\).

    Show Solution
    \[ 3.5\ \frac{\text{g}}{\text{cm}^3} \cdot \frac{1\ \text{kg}}{1000\ \text{g}} \cdot \left(\frac{100\ \text{cm}}{1\ \text{m}}\right)^3 = 3.5 \cdot \frac{1}{1000} \cdot (10^6) = 3500\ \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{m}^3} \]
    3. Dimensions and Dimensional Analysis

    Dimensions represent the type of quantity, not the unit.

    • Length: \([L]\)
    • Time: \([T]\)
    • Mass: \([M]\)

    Examples: [ \text{Velocity} : [L][T^{-1}] ] [ \text{Acceleration} : [L][T^{-2}] ] [ \text{Force} : [M][L][T^{-2}] ]

    You can use dimensions to check if an equation could possibly be correct.

    Suppose someone proposes:

    \[ v = 3t + 7x \]
    • Left-hand side: \(v \rightarrow [L/T]\)
    • Right-hand side:
    • \(3t \rightarrow [T]\)
    • \(7x \rightarrow [L]\)

    You cannot add \([T]\) and \([L]\).
    Therefore, the equation is nonsense physically.

    Dimensional analysis doesn‚Äôt prove an equation is right, but it can tell you if it‚Äôs definitely wrong.

    Often, we know: - the relevant quantities (e.g., \(g\), \(h\), \(v_0\)), and
    - the dimension we want (e.g., time [T])

    Dimensional analysis can guide us to plausible forms of relationships.

    It‚Äôs also very helpful in checking your work in exams.

    4. Position and Displacement Along a Line

    Position \(x(t)\) tells you where an object is along a 1D axis.
    Displacement is the change in position:

    \[ \Delta x = x_f - x_i \]

    It can be positive (forward), negative (backwards) or zero.

    From your Ch. 3 slides:

    Position depends on your choice of origin.
    If you choose \(x=0\) at one place vs another, the numeric values change, but physics does not.

    Displacement cares only about start and end, not the path taken.

    If \(x(t)\) is given as a function (e.g., a polynomial), we can: - compute displacement over a time interval: \(x(t_2)-x(t_1)\) - compute average velocity: \(\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\)

    Example:

    [ x(t) = 2t^2 - 4t + 1 ] From \(t=1\) to \(t=3\):

    \[ \Delta x = x(3)-x(1) \]
    Show Solution

    [ x(3)=2(9)-4(3)+1=18-12+1=7 ] [ x(1)=2(1)-4(1)+1=2-4+1=-1 ] [ \Delta x = 7 - (-1) = 8\ \text{m} ]

    5. Average vs Instantaneous Velocity

    Average velocity over a time interval:

    \[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \]

    Instantaneous velocity is the velocity at one instant:

    \[ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} \]

    On a position‚Äìtime graph: - average velocity ‚Üî slope of a secant line
    - instantaneous velocity ‚Üî slope of the tangent line

    From your slides:

    Average velocity is like ‚Äúoverall change‚Äù:
    ‚ÄúFrom 1:00 to 1:05, my position changed by 500 m, so on average I moved 100 m/min.‚Äù

    Instantaneous velocity zooms in on one time: ‚ÄúExactly at 1:02:30, what was my speed and direction?‚Äù

    Conceptual graph from your slides:

    Given: [ x(t) = t^3 - 4t + 2 ]

    • Average velocity from \(t=1\) to \(t=2\)
    • Instantaneous velocity at \(t=1\)
    Show Solution
    \[ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = 3t^2 - 4 \]

    Average from 1 to 2:

    [ x(2) = 8 - 8 + 2 = 2 ] [ x(1) = 1 - 4 + 2 = -1 ] [ \bar{v} = \frac{2 - (-1)}{2-1} = 3\ \text{m/s} ]

    Instantaneous at \(t=1\):

    \[ v(1) = 3(1)^2-4 = -1\ \text{m/s} \]

    Negative means motion is in the negative direction at that instant.

    6. Acceleration: Changing Velocity

    Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity:

    \[ a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} \]

    On a velocity‚Äìtime graph: - the slope is the acceleration

    Slide illustration:

    There are several ways velocity can change: - speed increases - speed decreases - direction reverses

    All of these involve acceleration, even if the speed is momentarily zero.

    For constant acceleration: [ a = \text{constant} \Rightarrow v(t) = v_0 + at,\quad x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 ]

    Suppose: [ a(t) = 6t,\quad v(0) = 3,\quad x(0)=0 ]

    Then: [ v(t) = \int 6t\,dt = 3t^2 + C ]

    Using \(v(0)=3\): [ 3(0)^2 + C = 3 \Rightarrow C = 3 ]

    \[ v(t) = 3t^2+3 \]

    Then: [ x(t) = \int (3t^2+3)\,dt = t^3 + 3t + C_2 ]

    Using \(x(0)=0\) gives \(C_2=0\).

    \[ x(t) = t^3 + 3t \]
    7. Free Fall & Constant Acceleration (1D Kinematics)

    Near Earth‚Äôs surface, ignoring air resistance:

    \[ a = g \approx 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2 \quad (\text{downward}) \]

    If we take upward as positive, then:

    \[ a = -g \]

    Kinematic equations (constant acceleration):

    [ v = v_0 + at ] [ x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 ] [ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0) ]

    Your slide visual:

    Free fall includes: - dropping an object (\(v_0 = 0\)) - throwing an object upward - throwing an object downward

    All share: - same acceleration magnitude (if air resistance is negligible) - parabolic position‚Äìtime graphs
    - linear velocity‚Äìtime graphs

    Example ‚Äî Dropped from 20 m

    \[ x_0 = 20\ \text{m},\quad v_0 = 0,\quad a=-9.8\ \text{m/s}^2 \]

    Position:

    \[ x(t) = 20 + 0\cdot t + \frac{1}{2}(-9.8)t^2 = 20 - 4.9 t^2 \]

    Time to hit the ground: set \(x(t)=0\):

    \[ 20 - 4.9t^2 = 0 \Rightarrow t^2=\frac{20}{4.9} \Rightarrow t \approx 2.02\ \text{s} \]

    Velocity on impact:

    \[ v = v_0 + at = -9.8(2.02) \approx -19.8\ \text{m/s} \]

    Negative indicates downward direction.

    8. Worked Examples (Tiered)

    Example 1 ‚Äî From Position to Velocity and Acceleration

    \[ x(t) = 4t^3 - 2t \]
    \[ v(t) = 12t^2 - 2 \]
    \[ a(t) = 24t \]

    Graph-style slide:

    • Position is cubic ‚Üí velocity quadratic ‚Üí acceleration linear.
    • As time increases:
    • velocity grows faster
    • acceleration keeps changing (not constant)

    Example 2 ‚Äî Car Accelerating from Rest

    A car starts from rest and accelerates at \(4\ \text{m/s}^2\) for 5 s.

    \[ v = v_0 + at = 0 + 4(5) = 20\ \text{m/s} \]
    \[ x = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 = 0 + 0 + \frac{1}{2}(4)(25) = 50\ \text{m} \]

    Example 3 ‚Äî Free-Fall Upward Throw

    A ball is thrown upward at \(12\ \text{m/s}\).
    Maximum height (using \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta x\) with final \(v=0\)):

    \[ 0 = (12)^2 + 2(-9.8)\Delta x \Rightarrow \Delta x = \frac{144}{19.6} \approx 7.35\ \text{m} \]
    9. Practice Problems (Try First, Then Expand)
    1. Given \(x(t)=2t^3 - 5t^2 + 7\), find \(v(t)\) and \(a(t)\).
    2. A car accelerates from rest at \(4\ \text{m/s}^2\) for 5 s. Find distance traveled.
    3. A ball is thrown upward at \(12\ \text{m/s}\). Find max height.
    4. Convert 45 mph to m/s.
    5. If \(v(t)=6t\) and \(x(0)=0\), find \(x(t)\).

    Think for each: - What is given? (position? velocity? acceleration?)
    - Do I need a derivative or integral?
    - Is this constant acceleration?

    Problem 1 Solution

    [ x(t)=2t^3 - 5t^2 + 7 ] [ v(t)=\frac{dx}{dt}=6t^2 - 10t ] [ a(t)=\frac{dv}{dt}=12t - 10 ]

    Problem 2 Solution
    \[ x = \frac{1}{2}at^2 = \frac{1}{2}(4)(25)=50\ \text{m} \]
    Problem 3 Solution
    \[ h = \frac{v_0^2}{2g} = \frac{144}{19.6} \approx 7.35\ \text{m} \]
    Problem 4 Solution
    \[ 45\ \text{mph} \approx 20.1\ \text{m/s} \]
    Problem 5 Solution

    [ v(t)=6t \Rightarrow x(t)=\int 6t\,dt = 3t^2 + C ] Using \(x(0)=0\) gives \(C=0\). Thus: [ x(t)=3t^2 ]
