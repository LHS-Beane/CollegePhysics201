    üìò 1. Units, Dimensions, and Basic Concepts¶

    SI Base Units¶

    Quantity Unit Symbol
    Length meter m
    Mass kilogram kg
    Time second s
    Temperature kelvin K
    Electric current ampere A

    Dimensions¶

    Dimensions express the type of quantity.

    Examples:

    \[ \text{Velocity}: [L][T^{-1}] \]
    \[ \text{Acceleration}: [L][T^{-2}] \]
    \[ \text{Force}: [M][L][T^{-2}] \]

    üìå If dimensions don‚Äôt match on both sides of an equation, the equation is wrong.

    ‚ú® Example: Dimensional Analysis¶

    Is
    [ v = 3t + 7x ]
    valid?

    • Left side: \([L/T]\)
    • Right side: \(3t \rightarrow [T]\), \(7x \rightarrow [L]\)

    ‚ùå Invalid. Units don‚Äôt match, so this equation cannot describe motion.

    üîß Unit Conversions (Factor-Label Method)¶

    Example:

    Convert 60 mph ‚Üí m/s

    \[ 60\frac{\text{mi}}{\text{hr}} \cdot \frac{1609\ \text{m}}{1\ \text{mi}} \cdot \frac{1\ \text{hr}}{3600\ \text{s}} = 26.8\ \text{m/s} \]

    Unit consistency = physics success.

    üìò 2. Position, Velocity, and Acceleration¶

    Definitions¶

    \[ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} \]
    \[ a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} \]

    These come directly from calculus.

    • Velocity = rate of change of position
    • Acceleration = rate of change of velocity

    üîé Example Function¶

    Let:

    \[ x(t) = t^3 - 4t + 2 \]

    Then:

    \[ v(t) = 3t^2 - 4 \]
    \[ a(t) = 6t \]

    Interpretation:

    • Velocity changes quadratically
    • Acceleration increases linearly ‚Üí motion speeds up faster over time

    üìò 3. Derivatives in Kinematics¶

    Derivatives describe instantaneous rates of change.

    Example (Try This!)¶

    \[ x(t) = 5t^2 - 3t + 8 \]

    Velocity:

    \[ v(t) = 10t - 3 \]

    Acceleration:

    \[ a(t) = 10 \]

    Acceleration is constant ‚Üí parabola position + linear velocity.

    üìò 4. Integrals in Kinematics¶

    Integrals reconstruct motion when velocity or acceleration is known.

    If acceleration is known:¶

    \[ v(t) = \int a(t)\, dt \]

    If velocity is known:¶

    \[ x(t) = \int v(t)\, dt \]

    üåü Constant Acceleration (Very Important)¶

    If acceleration \(a\) is constant:

    \[ v(t) = v_0 + at \]
    \[ x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

    These will appear everywhere in physics.

    üåü Non-Constant Acceleration¶

    Example:

    \[ a(t) = 4t \]
    \[ v(t) = \int 4t\,dt = 2t^2 + C \]

    Use initial conditions to solve for \(C\).

    üìò 5. Free Fall¶

    In free fall, acceleration is constant:

    \[ a = g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2 \]

    Velocity:

    \[ v(t) = gt + v_0 \]

    Position:

    \[ x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \]

    If dropped:
    [ v_0 = 0 ]

    üìò 6. Graph Interpretation¶

    Understanding graphs is one of the MOST important physics skills.

    üìà Position ‚Üí Velocity¶

    Velocity = slope of position graph.

    üìâ Velocity ‚Üí Acceleration¶

    Acceleration = slope of velocity graph.

    üß≠ Velocity ‚Üí Displacement¶

    Displacement = area under velocity graph.

    üéì 7. Worked Examples¶

    Example 1 ‚Äî Derivatives from Position¶

    \[ x(t) = 4t^3 - 2t \]
    \[ v(t) = 12t^2 - 2 \]
    \[ a(t) = 24t \]

    Example 2 ‚Äî Dropped Object¶

    A ball is dropped from 20 m.

    Velocity after 2 s:¶

    \[ v = gt = 9.8(2) = 19.6\ \text{m/s} \]

    Position:¶

    \[ x = 20 - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(2^2) = 0.4\ \text{m} \]

    Just above the ground.

    Example 3 ‚Äî Integrating Non-Constant Acceleration¶

    \[ a(t)=6t,\quad v(0)=3 \]
    \[ v(t)=3t^2 + 3 \]
    \[ x(t)=t^3 + 3t \]
