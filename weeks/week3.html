Week 3

Newton’s Laws of Motion

This week moves from describing motion to explaining it. We connect forces to acceleration, build and interpret free-body diagrams, and apply Newton’s three laws to 1D and 2D motion, including friction and multi-object systems.

● Topic: Dynamics & Forces ● Prereqs: Week 1–2 (kinematics & vectors)
Learning Objectives ▾

By the end of this week, you should be able to:

  • State Newton’s three laws of motion and interpret them physically.
  • Identify all forces acting on an object and draw a correct free-body diagram (FBD).
  • Apply ∑F = ma in one and two dimensions.
  • Resolve forces into components and choose appropriate coordinate axes.
  • Analyze motion on horizontal surfaces and inclines with and without friction.
  • Solve problems involving tension, normal forces, springs, and multi-object systems.
  • Recognize action–reaction pairs and avoid common misconceptions.
Core Notes: Newton’s Laws & Types of Forces ▾

3.1 Newton’s First Law (Inertia)

Newton’s First Law: An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion remains in motion with constant velocity unless acted on by a net external force.

  • Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion.
  • Mass is a measure of inertia.
  • If ∑F = 0, the object is in equilibrium: it either remains at rest or moves with constant velocity.

First Law is a special case of Second Law when ∑F = 0 ⇒ a = 0.

3.2 Newton’s Second Law

Newton’s Second Law connects force and motion:

∑⃗F = m⃗a    (vector form)

  • The net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on an object.
  • In 1D, we can write ∑Fx = m ax.
  • In 2D, we split into components:
    ∑Fx = m ax and ∑Fy = m ay.
  • Mass is constant in this course; acceleration is proportional to net force.

Weight: The force of gravity near Earth’s surface is W = mg, directed downward.

Be careful to distinguish mass (kg, intrinsic) from weight (N, force due to gravity).

3.3 Newton’s Third Law (Action–Reaction)

Newton’s Third Law: For every force that object A exerts on object B, object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

Symbolically: if ⃗FA→B is the force of A on B, then ⃗FB→A = −⃗FA→B.

  • Action–reaction forces always act on different objects.
  • They are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
  • They do not cancel in a single object’s free-body diagram (you only draw forces on one object).

Common example: You push on a wall; the wall pushes back on you with equal and opposite force.

3.4 Common Forces in Mechanics

In typical problems, you will encounter the following forces:

  • Weight W = mg, downward.
  • Normal force N: contact force perpendicular to the surface.
  • Tension T: pulling force along an ideal rope or string.
  • Friction:
    • Static friction: fs ≤ μs N, adjusts up to a maximum.
    • Kinetic friction: fk = μk N, opposes motion.
  • Spring force: Fs = −k x, opposite displacement.
  • Applied forces: pushes/pulls at some angle relative to a surface.

The key to solving dynamics problems is: identify forces → draw a free-body diagram → choose axes → resolve components → apply ∑F = ma.

Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs) ▾

A free-body diagram shows all forces acting on a single object, isolated from its surroundings.

  1. Choose the object of interest.
  2. Represent it as a simple shape (box, dot).
  3. Identify all forces acting on the object (not by the object).
  4. Draw each force as an arrow starting at the object, labeled with type and magnitude.
  5. Choose coordinate axes (often along/normal to the surface if inclines are involved).

Example: Block of mass m on a rough horizontal surface with an applied force F to the right:

  • Weight: W = mg downward.
  • Normal force: N upward.
  • Applied force: F to the right.
  • Friction: f to the left (if motion or tendency to move right).
Worked Examples ▾

Example 1: Horizontal Pull (No Friction)

A student pulls a 5.0 kg box on a frictionless floor with a horizontal force of 12 N. Find the acceleration.

Solution (outline)

  1. Draw an FBD: forces are weight mg down, normal N up, pull F = 12 N to the right.
  2. Vertical: N − mg = 0 ⇒ N = mg. No vertical acceleration.
  3. Horizontal: ∑Fx = F = m ax.
  4. ax = F / m = 12 / 5.0 = 2.4 m/s² to the right.

Example 2: Inclined Plane with Friction

A 10 kg block slides down a 30° incline with kinetic friction coefficient μk = 0.20. Find the acceleration.

Solution (outline)

  1. Choose axes: x parallel to the incline (down), y perpendicular.
  2. Forces: weight mg (down), normal N (perpendicular), friction fk = μk N up the plane.
  3. Components of weight:
    • mg sinθ down the plane
    • mg cosθ into the plane
  4. Normal direction: N − mg cosθ = 0 ⇒ N = mg cosθ.
  5. Along the plane (taking down the plane as positive):
    ∑Fx = mg sinθ − fk = mg sinθ − μk mg cosθ = m a.
  6. Cancel m:
    a = g (sinθ − μk cosθ).
  7. Plug in numbers with g ≈ 9.8 m/s², θ = 30°:
    a ≈ 9.8 (0.5 − 0.2 · 0.866) ≈ 9.8 (0.5 − 0.173) ≈ 9.8 · 0.327 ≈ 3.2 m/s² down the plane.

Example 3: Two-Block System (Tension)

Two blocks, m₁ = 2.0 kg and m₂ = 3.0 kg, are connected by a light string and pulled across a frictionless surface by a horizontal 20 N force applied to m₂. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the string.

Solution (outline)

  1. Treat both masses together to find acceleration:
    Total mass M = m₁ + m₂ = 5.0 kg.
  2. ∑Fext = 20 N = M a ⇒ a = 20 / 5.0 = 4.0 m/s² (to the right).
  3. Now isolate m₁. Forces on m₁ in x-direction: tension T to the right.
  4. ∑Fx = T = m₁ a ⇒ T = 2.0 · 4.0 = 8.0 N.

Example 4: Elevator Apparent Weight

A 70 kg student stands on a scale in an elevator. The elevator accelerates upward at 2.0 m/s². What does the scale read?

Solution (outline)

  1. FBD of student: normal force N up (scale reading), weight mg down.
  2. Take upward as positive: ∑Fy = N − mg = m a.
  3. N = m(g + a).
  4. Compute: N = 70 (9.8 + 2.0) ≈ 70 · 11.8 ≈ 826 N.
  5. The scale “weight” is 826 N, larger than true weight because of the upward acceleration.
Practice Problems ▾

Try these before looking at the solutions. Draw an FBD for each.

  1. A 4.0 kg block is pushed with a constant 10 N horizontal force across a frictionless surface. Find the acceleration.
  2. A 12 kg box is on a horizontal surface with μk = 0.25. A horizontal 40 N force is applied. Find the acceleration.
  3. A 15 kg crate rests on a rough horizontal floor with μs = 0.40. What is the minimum horizontal force required to start it moving?
  4. A 5.0 kg block sits on a 25° incline with no friction. Find the acceleration of the block.
  5. A 2.0 kg block is attached to a 3.0 kg hanging mass over a frictionless pulley (Atwood-style system). Assume the 3.0 kg mass hangs freely. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the string.
  6. A 60 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator that accelerates downward at 1.5 m/s². What does the scale read?
  7. A 10 kg block is pulled up a 30° incline with a force of 80 N parallel to the incline. Coefficient of kinetic friction is μk = 0.10. Find the acceleration up the plane.
  8. Identify the action–reaction pair: a book rests on a table under Earth’s gravity. List all forces on the book and all forces on the Earth involved in the Third Law pair.
Practice Problem Solutions (Outline) ▾
  1. 4.0 kg, 10 N, frictionless:
    a = F / m = 10 / 4.0 = 2.5 m/s².
  2. 12 kg, μk = 0.25, F = 40 N:
    Normal N = mg, friction fk = μk N = 0.25 · 12 · 9.8 ≈ 29.4 N. Net force: F − fk ≈ 40 − 29.4 = 10.6 N.
    a ≈ 10.6 / 12 ≈ 0.88 m/s².
  3. Static friction to start motion:
    Max static friction fs,max = μs N = 0.40 · 12 · 9.8 ≈ 47 N.
    Minimum horizontal force ≈ 47 N.
  4. Frictionless 25° incline:
    a = g sinθ = 9.8 sin 25° ≈ 9.8 · 0.423 ≈ 4.1 m/s² down the plane.
  5. 2.0 kg on table, 3.0 kg hanging:
    Let system accelerate with a, heavier mass going down.
    For 3.0 kg: mg − T = m a.
    For 2.0 kg: T = m a.
    Combine: 3g − T = 3a and T = 2a ⇒ 3g − 2a = 3a ⇒ 3g = 5a ⇒ a = (3/5) g ≈ 0.6 · 9.8 ≈ 5.9 m/s².
    Tension: T = 2a ≈ 11.8 N.
  6. Elevator downward, 60 kg, a = 1.5 m/s²:
    Take upward positive: N − mg = m a (but now a is negative).
    N = m(g + a) with a = −1.5 ⇒ N = 60 (9.8 − 1.5) = 60 · 8.3 ≈ 498 N.
  7. 10 kg up 30° incline, F = 80 N, μk = 0.10:
    Components: weight down plane mg sinθ, friction down plane fk = μk N, N = mg cosθ.
    Along plane (up positive): ∑F = F − mg sinθ − μk mg cosθ = m a.
    Plug numbers (with g = 9.8, θ = 30°):
    a ≈ [80 − 10·9.8·0.5 − 0.10·10·9.8·0.866] / 10
    ≈ [80 − 49 − 8.5] / 10 ≈ 22.5 / 10 ≈ 2.3 m/s² up the plane.
  8. Action–reaction pair (book on table):
    Forces on the book:
    • Normal force from table on book (upward).
    • Weight of Earth on book (downward).
    Action–reaction examples:
    • Book on Earth: Earth pulls book down (book’s weight); book pulls Earth up with equal and opposite gravitational force.
    • Table on book: table pushes up on book; book pushes down on table.
