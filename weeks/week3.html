Newton’s Laws of Motion & Free-Body Diagrams

This module introduces dynamics: how forces cause changes in motion. You will learn to identify forces, build free-body diagrams, and apply Newton’s three laws in 1D and 2D, including friction, tension, and multi-object systems.

Learning Goals

  • State and interpret Newton’s First, Second, and Third Laws.
  • Identify all forces acting on a system and draw a correct free-body diagram (FBD).
  • Apply $\sum \vec{F} = m \vec{a}$ in component form in 1D and 2D.
  • Analyze motion on horizontal surfaces and inclines with and without friction.
  • Solve problems involving tension, normal force, friction, and multi-object systems.
  • Recognize action–reaction pairs and avoid common misconceptions about “cancelling forces.”

How to Use This Module

  • Work through the Notes section first; treat it as your mini-textbook chapter.
  • Study the blueprint-style FBD diagrams and redraw them in your notebook.
  • Follow the Worked Examples step by step, paying attention to setup.
  • Attempt all Practice Problems before opening the solution outlines.
  • Use the Interactive Panel to connect force, mass, and acceleration numerically.

1. Newton’s Three Laws of Motion

1.1 First Law – Inertia

An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues with constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.

  • Inertia: tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion.
  • Mass: measure of inertia (more mass → harder to change velocity).
  • Equilibrium: $\sum \vec{F} = 0 \Rightarrow \vec{a} = \vec{0}$, so velocity is constant (could be zero).

1.2 Second Law – Dynamics

The net force on an object equals mass times acceleration:

\[ \sum \vec{F} = m \vec{a}. \]

  • Net force is the vector sum of all forces acting on the object.
  • In 1D: $\sum F_x = m a_x$.
  • In 2D: $\sum F_x = m a_x$ and $\sum F_y = m a_y$.
  • Mass is constant in this course, so changing the net force changes the acceleration.

1.3 Third Law – Action & Reaction

For every force that object A exerts on B, there is an equal and opposite force that B exerts on A:

\[ \vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\,\vec{F}_{B \to A}. \]

  • The pair always involves two objects.
  • They act on different objects, so they never cancel in a single free-body diagram.
  • Example: you push on the wall; the wall pushes back on you with equal magnitude, opposite direction.

2. Common Forces and Free-Body Diagrams

In mechanics, you’ll repeatedly encounter a small set of force types:

  • Weight $W = mg$ (downward, toward Earth’s center).
  • Normal force $N$ (perpendicular contact force from a surface).
  • Tension $T$ (pulling force along an ideal rope or string).
  • Friction $f$ (parallel to the surface, opposes relative motion or tendency).
  • Spring force $F_s = -k x$ (restoring force opposite displacement from equilibrium).
  • Applied forces $F_{\text{app}}$ at some angle.

Recipe for a free-body diagram (FBD):

  1. Isolate the object of interest.
  2. Represent it as a dot or box.
  3. Draw an arrow for every force acting on the object, labeled and starting at the object.
  4. Choose axes (often along/normal to the surface).
  5. Write $\sum F_x = m a_x$ and $\sum F_y = m a_y$ using components.

Blueprint-style FBD for a block on a rough horizontal surface pulled to the right.

3. Components, Inclines, and Friction

For many problems, choosing axes aligned with the motion simplifies the math, especially on inclines.

3.1 Weight Components on an Incline

For a block of mass $m$ on an incline at angle $\theta$:

  • Component perpendicular to plane: $mg \cos\theta$.
  • Component parallel to plane (down-slope): $mg \sin\theta$.

3.2 Friction Models

  • Static friction adjusts up to a maximum: \[ f_s \le \mu_s N. \]
  • Kinetic friction (sliding): \[ f_k = \mu_k N. \]
  • Direction: opposite the direction of motion (or intended motion along the surface).

3.3 Newton’s Second Law on an Incline (with friction)

Choose $x$ along the plane (down-slope positive), $y$ perpendicular to the plane (out of surface positive).

\[ \sum F_x = mg \sin\theta - f_k = m a, \quad \sum F_y = N - mg \cos\theta = 0. \]

Using $f_k = \mu_k N = \mu_k mg \cos\theta$, \[ a = g\big(\sin\theta - \mu_k \cos\theta\big). \]

4. Multi-Object Systems and Tension

Many Newton’s laws problems involve more than one object connected by ropes or in contact. Strategy:

  1. Draw an FBD for each object separately.
  2. Write $\sum F = m a$ for each object (same $a$ if rope is massless & inextensible).
  3. Relate tensions and accelerations through constraints (e.g., same magnitude of tension in a massless rope).
  4. Solve the system of equations for unknowns (accelerations, tensions, etc.).

Example pattern: Two blocks on a horizontal surface

For two blocks $m_1$ and $m_2$ pulled by a force $F$:

  • Treat $m_1 + m_2$ as one system to quickly find acceleration: $a = F/(m_1 + m_2)$.
  • Then isolate one block to find internal tension using $T = m a$.

For Atwood-style setups (one mass hanging, one on a surface), the same logic applies: consistent acceleration but different force equations for each object.

5. Summary of Key Relationships

  • First Law describes equilibrium ($\sum \vec{F} = 0$); Second Law describes general motion ($\sum \vec{F} = m \vec{a}$).
  • Third Law: forces between two objects come in equal-and-opposite pairs on different objects.
  • Weight: $W = mg$; normal: $N$ is perpendicular contact; friction: $f_s \le \mu_s N$, $f_k = \mu_k N$.
  • On inclines, decompose weight into $mg \sin\theta$ (parallel) and $mg \cos\theta$ (perpendicular).
  • Dynamics problems are solved by: FBD → components → $\sum F_x = m a_x$, $\sum F_y = m a_y$ → solve.
  • For multi-object systems, write equations for each object and use constraints (same $a$, same $T$ in an ideal rope).

Example 1: Horizontal Pull with Friction

A 10 kg block is pulled to the right by a horizontal force of $F = 40\ \text{N}$ on a rough horizontal surface. The coefficient of kinetic friction is $\mu_k = 0.30$.

  1. Draw a free-body diagram.
  2. Find the friction force.
  3. Compute the acceleration of the block.
Show Solution

(1) FBD: Forces: weight $mg$ down, normal $N$ up, pull $F$ right, friction $f_k$ left.

(2) Normal & friction:

Vertical equilibrium (no vertical acceleration): \[ N - mg = 0 \Rightarrow N = mg = 10 \cdot 9.8 = 98\ \text{N}. \]

Kinetic friction: \[ f_k = \mu_k N = 0.30 \cdot 98 \approx 29.4\ \text{N}. \]

(3) Horizontal dynamics:

Take right as positive: \[ \sum F_x = F - f_k = m a. \] So \[ a = \frac{F - f_k}{m} = \frac{40 - 29.4}{10} \approx 1.06\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

Example 2: Block on an Incline with Friction

A 12 kg crate is on a $30^\circ$ incline. The coefficient of kinetic friction is $\mu_k = 0.20$. The crate is released from rest and slides down. Find its acceleration.

Show Solution

Step 1: Choose axes & FBD.

  • $x$-axis along the incline (down-slope positive), $y$-axis perpendicular to the incline.
  • Forces: $mg$ downward, normal $N$ perpendicular to plane, friction $f_k$ up the plane.

Step 2: Components and normal force.

Parallel component: $mg \sin\theta$; perpendicular: $mg \cos\theta$.

$\sum F_y = 0 \Rightarrow N - mg \cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow N = mg \cos\theta.$

Step 3: Along the plane.

\[ \sum F_x = mg \sin\theta - f_k = m a. \] With $f_k = \mu_k N = \mu_k mg \cos\theta$: \[ a = g (\sin\theta - \mu_k \cos\theta). \]

Plug numbers: $g = 9.8$, $\theta = 30^\circ$: \[ a \approx 9.8 \left(0.5 - 0.20 \cdot 0.866\right) = 9.8 (0.5 - 0.173) \approx 9.8 \cdot 0.327 \approx 3.2\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

Example 3: Two-Block System with Tension

Two blocks are connected by a light string on a frictionless surface. Block 1 has mass $m_1 = 2.0\ \text{kg}$, block 2 has mass $m_2 = 3.0\ \text{kg}$. A horizontal force of $F = 20\ \text{N}$ pulls on $m_2$.

  1. Find the acceleration of the system.
  2. Find the tension in the string between the blocks.
Show Solution

(1) Acceleration of the system.

Treat $m_1 + m_2$ as one combined mass: \[ \sum F = F = (m_1 + m_2) a. \] So \[ a = \frac{F}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{20}{2.0 + 3.0} = \frac{20}{5} = 4.0\ \text{m/s}^2. \]

(2) Tension in the string.

Now isolate $m_1$. The only horizontal force on $m_1$ is tension $T$: \[ \sum F_x = T = m_1 a \Rightarrow T = 2.0 \cdot 4.0 = 8.0\ \text{N}. \]

Practice Set – Week 3

Try each problem without looking at the outline. Draw clean free-body diagrams and label all forces.

Problem 1 – Identifying Forces

A book rests on a table. The Earth pulls down on the book with gravitational force $mg$.

  1. List all forces acting on the book and draw an FBD.
  2. Identify one Newton’s Third Law pair involving the book.

Solution (outline):

Forces on the book: weight $mg$ downward, normal force $N$ upward from the table.

Third Law pair example: Earth pulls on the book (downward); book pulls on Earth (upward) with equal magnitude.

Problem 2 – Friction Threshold

A 20 kg crate is at rest on a horizontal floor. The coefficient of static friction is $\mu_s = 0.40$. A horizontal force $F$ is gradually increased.

  1. Find the minimum $F$ required to start the crate moving.
  2. Draw the FBD at the threshold of motion.

Solution (outline):

$N = mg = 20 \cdot 9.8 = 196\ \text{N}$.

Maximum static friction: \[ f_{s,\max} = \mu_s N = 0.40 \cdot 196 \approx 78\ \text{N}. \] Threshold force $F_{\min} \approx 78\ \text{N}$.

Problem 3 – Elevator Apparent Weight

A 65 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator. The elevator accelerates upward at $a = 1.8\ \text{m/s}^2$.

  1. Draw an FBD for the person.
  2. Find the scale reading (normal force).

Solution (outline):

Take upward positive: $\sum F_y = N - mg = m a$.

So $N = m(g + a) = 65(9.8 + 1.8) \approx 65 \cdot 11.6 \approx 754\ \text{N}$.

Problem 4 – Atwood-Style System

A 2.0 kg block rests on a frictionless table and is connected by a light string over a frictionless pulley to a 3.0 kg hanging mass.

  1. Draw FBDs for each mass.
  2. Find the magnitude of the acceleration of the system.
  3. Find the tension in the string.

Solution (outline):

Let the 3.0 kg mass move downward with acceleration $a$, and the 2.0 kg block move right with $a$.

For 3.0 kg: $mg - T = m a$ → $3g - T = 3a$.
For 2.0 kg: $T = 2a$.

Substitute: $3g - 2a = 3a \Rightarrow 3g = 5a \Rightarrow a = (3/5)g \approx 5.9\ \text{m/s}^2$, and $T = 2a \approx 11.8\ \text{N}$.
