Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

This module treats simple harmonic motion as a differential equation, connects it to circular motion, and analyzes the energy of oscillating systems such as mass–spring oscillators and simple pendulums.

Learning Goals

  • Recognize the conditions under which a system executes simple harmonic motion (SHM).
  • Write and interpret the SHM differential equation \(\dfrac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -\omega^2 x\).
  • Use calculus to derive \(x(t)\), \(v(t)\), and \(a(t)\) for SHM.
  • Relate SHM to uniform circular motion via projection.
  • Analyze energy in SHM: potential, kinetic, and total mechanical energy.
  • Apply SHM to mass–spring systems and the small-angle approximation of a simple pendulum.

How to Use This Module

  • Work through the Notes carefully; treat them as a mini-chapter on SHM.
  • Use the diagrams to connect equations to physical pictures (spring, circle, pendulum).
  • Study the Worked Examples and replicate the steps on paper.
  • Attempt the Practice Problems before revealing solution outlines.
  • Experiment with the Interactive Panel to see how changing \(m, k, x_0, v_0\) alters \(A\), \(\omega\), and the graph.

1. What Is Simple Harmonic Motion?

A system executes simple harmonic motion (SHM) when:

  • There is a stable equilibrium position.
  • The restoring force is proportional to the displacement from equilibrium and directed toward equilibrium.

For a mass–spring system on a frictionless surface: \[ F = -kx, \] where \(k\) is the spring constant and \(x\) is the displacement from equilibrium.

Newton’s 2nd law gives \[ m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -kx, \] or \[ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -\frac{k}{m} x \equiv -\omega^2 x, \] where \[ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \] is the angular frequency.

2. General Solution to the SHM Equation

The differential equation \[ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -\omega^2 x \] has the general sinusoidal solution \[ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), \] where:

  • \(A\) is the amplitude (maximum displacement).
  • \(\omega\) is the angular frequency.
  • \(\phi\) is the phase constant, set by initial conditions.

The period and frequency are: \[ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}, \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{\omega}{2\pi}. \]

3. Velocity and Acceleration from Calculus

Starting from \[ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), \] we differentiate to find velocity and acceleration.

Velocity: \[ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = -A\omega \sin(\omega t + \phi). \]

Acceleration: \[ a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = -A\omega^2 \cos(\omega t + \phi) = -\omega^2 x(t). \]

Note that acceleration is always proportional to \(-x\), pointing toward equilibrium.

4. Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

For a mass–spring system with amplitude \(A\), the total mechanical energy is \[ E = \tfrac12 kA^2. \]

At a general displacement \(x\):

  • Potential energy: \[ U(x) = \tfrac12 kx^2. \]
  • Kinetic energy: \[ K(x) = E - U(x) = \tfrac12 kA^2 - \tfrac12 kx^2 = \tfrac12 k(A^2 - x^2). \]

The particle speeds up where potential energy is low (near \(x=0\)) and slows down where potential energy is high (near \(x = \pm A\)).

5. SHM as Projection of Circular Motion

SHM can be viewed as the projection of uniform circular motion onto one axis. Imagine a point moving on a circle of radius \(A\) with angular speed \(\omega\).

The horizontal coordinate is \[ x(t) = A\cos(\omega t), \] which is exactly the SHM solution.

6. The Simple Pendulum (Small-Angle Approximation)

A pendulum of length \(L\) and bob mass \(m\) swings under gravity. For small angular displacements \(\theta\) (in radians), the motion approximates SHM.

The equation of motion for small angles is \[ \frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = -\frac{g}{L}\theta, \] which has the same form as SHM with \[ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}, \quad T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}. \]

Example 1 – Finding SHM Parameters from Initial Conditions

A mass–spring system has \(m = 0.50\,\text{kg}\) and \(k = 8.0\,\text{N/m}\). At \(t = 0\), the mass is at \(x_0 = 0.15\,\text{m}\) and has zero velocity.

  1. Find \(\omega\), the angular frequency.
  2. Find the amplitude \(A\) and phase constant \(\phi\).
  3. Write the equation of motion \(x(t)\).
  4. Find the period \(T\).
Show Solution

(1) \[ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{8.0}{0.50}} = \sqrt{16} = 4.0\,\text{rad/s}. \]

(2) At \(t=0\), \[ x(0) = A\cos\phi = x_0 = 0.15\,\text{m}, \quad v(0) = -A\omega\sin\phi = 0. \] The second equation implies \(\sin\phi = 0\Rightarrow \phi = 0\) (choose the case consistent with \(x_0>0\)). Then \(A = x_0 = 0.15\,\text{m}.\)

(3) \[ x(t) = 0.15\cos(4.0t). \]

(4) \[ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{4.0} = \frac{\pi}{2} \approx 1.57\,\text{s}. \]

Example 2 – Energy and Speed at a Displacement

For the system in Example 1, find the speed of the mass when its displacement is \(x = 0.10\,\text{m}\).

Show Solution

Total energy: \[ E = \tfrac12 kA^2 = \tfrac12 (8.0)(0.15^2) = 4.0 \times 0.0225 = 0.09\,\text{J}. \]

At displacement \(x\), \[ U = \tfrac12 kx^2 = \tfrac12 (8.0)(0.10^2) = 4.0 \times 0.01 = 0.04\,\text{J}. \]

Then \(K = E - U = 0.09 - 0.04 = 0.05\,\text{J}\).

Kinetic energy is \(K = \tfrac12 m v^2\Rightarrow v = \sqrt{\frac{2K}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(0.05)}{0.50}} = \sqrt{0.2} \approx 0.45\,\text{m/s}. \]

Example 3 – Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum of length \(L = 0.80\,\text{m}\) is released from rest at a small angle of \(\theta_0 = 5^\circ\) (convert to radians). Assuming the small-angle approximation, find:

  1. The angular frequency \(\omega\).
  2. The period \(T\).
  3. An expression for \(\theta(t)\).
Show Solution

(1) \[ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}} = \sqrt{\frac{9.8}{0.80}} \approx \sqrt{12.25} \approx 3.50\,\text{rad/s}. \]

(2) \[ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} \approx \frac{2\pi}{3.50} \approx 1.79\,\text{s}. \]

(3) Since it is released from rest at maximum angle, we can take \(\theta(t) = \theta_0\cos(\omega t)\). Convert \(5^\circ\) to radians: \(\theta_0 \approx 5\pi/180 \approx 0.087\,\text{rad}\).

Thus \[ \theta(t) \approx 0.087\cos(3.50 t). \]

Practice Set – Simple Harmonic Motion

Work through these problems to strengthen your understanding. Try them without notes first, then use the outlines to check your reasoning.

Problem 1 – SHM from a DE

A particle along the x-axis satisfies \[ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -9x. \]

  1. Identify \(\omega\).
  2. Write the general solution for \(x(t)\).
  3. If \(x(0) = 0\) and \(v(0) = 3.0\,\text{m/s}\), find the specific solution.

Solution (outline):

\(\omega^2 = 9 \Rightarrow \omega = 3\,\text{rad/s}.\)

General solution: \[ x(t) = A\cos(3t) + B\sin(3t) \] or equivalently \(x(t) = C\cos(3t + \phi)\).

Use initial conditions: \(x(0)=A=0\Rightarrow A=0\); \(v(t) = -3A\sin(3t)+3B\cos(3t)\). Then \(v(0)=3B=3.0\Rightarrow B=1.0.\)

So \(x(t) = \sin(3t)\,\text{m}.\)

Problem 2 – Energy at Turning Point

A mass–spring system has amplitude \(A = 0.20\,\text{m}\) and spring constant \(k = 12\,\text{N/m}\). The mass is \(m = 0.60\,\text{kg}\).

  1. Find the total energy of the system.
  2. What are the kinetic and potential energies at \(x = 0.10\,\text{m}\)?

Solution (outline):

\(E = \tfrac12 kA^2 = \tfrac12(12)(0.20^2) = 6 \cdot 0.04 = 0.24\,\text{J}.\)

At \(x=0.10\,\text{m}\), \[ U = \tfrac12 kx^2 = 6 \cdot 0.01 = 0.06\,\text{J}, \quad K = E - U = 0.18\,\text{J}. \]

Problem 3 – Mixed Initial Conditions

A mass–spring oscillator has \(m = 1.0\,\text{kg}\), \(k = 25\,\text{N/m}\). At \(t=0\), the mass is at \(x_0 = 0.10\,\text{m}\) and has velocity \(v_0 = 0.50\,\text{m/s}\).

  1. Find \(\omega\).
  2. Determine the amplitude \(A\).
  3. Find the phase constant \(\phi\) in \(x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)\).

Solution (outline):

\(\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{25/1} = 5\,\text{rad/s}.\)

Use relationships at \(t=0\): \[ x_0 = A\cos\phi,\quad v_0 = -A\omega\sin\phi. \]

Then \[ A^2 = x_0^2 + \left(\frac{v_0}{\omega}\right)^2 = 0.10^2 + (0.50/5)^2 = 0.01 + 0.01 = 0.02, \] so \(A = \sqrt{0.02} \approx 0.141\,\text{m}.\)

\(\cos\phi = x_0/A \approx 0.10/0.141 \approx 0.707\Rightarrow \phi\approx \pi/4\) (check sign with \(v_0\)).

Problem 4 – Pendulum Period Comparison

Two simple pendulums have lengths \(L_1 = 0.50\,\text{m}\) and \(L_2 = 2.0\,\text{m}\).

  1. Find the ratio of their periods \(T_2/T_1\).
  2. Which one swings more slowly?

Solution (outline):

\(T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\Rightarrow T_2/T_1 = \sqrt{L_2/L_1} = \sqrt{2.0/0.50} = \sqrt{4} = 2.\)

The longer pendulum (\(L_2\)) swings more slowly and has double the period.
