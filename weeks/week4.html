Work, Energy, and Power

This module develops the work–energy theorem and conservation of mechanical energy using calculus. You’ll connect forces and motion to energy diagrams, interpret $F$–$x$ graphs, and apply power and efficiency ideas to real mechanical systems.

Learning Goals

  • Define work using the dot product and interpret it geometrically.
  • Compute work done by constant and variable forces, including from $F$–$x$ graphs.
  • Derive the work–energy theorem and kinetic energy expression from calculus.
  • Use potential energy functions ($U_g$, $U_s$) and conservation of mechanical energy.
  • Distinguish conservative and non-conservative forces and use $W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta E_{\text{mech}}$.
  • Apply power and efficiency to real systems (motors, humans, lifting, braking).

How to Use This Module

  • Read the Notes like a short chapter, working through each equation.
  • Study the blueprint diagrams to visualize work, energy, and power in mechanical systems.
  • Follow the Worked Examples slowly, focusing on modeling and setup.
  • Do the Practice Problems on paper before checking solution outlines.
  • Use the Interactive Panel to play with energy conversion and losses.

1. Work and the Dot Product

Work measures how a force changes the energy of an object by acting through a displacement.

For a constant force $\vec F$ acting while the object undergoes displacement $\Delta \vec r$,

\[ W = \vec F \cdot \Delta \vec r = F \Delta r \cos\theta, \] where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec F$ and $\Delta \vec r$.

Sign of work:

  • $W > 0$ if force has a component along the displacement (adds energy).
  • $W < 0$ if force has a component opposite the displacement (removes energy).
  • $W = 0$ if $\vec F$ is perpendicular to $\Delta \vec r$ (no energy transfer by that force).

2. Work from Variable Forces and $F(x)$ Graphs

If the force changes with position, $F = F(x)$, we use an integral:

\[ W = \int_{x_i}^{x_f} F(x)\, dx. \]

Geometrically, work is the signed area under the $F$–$x$ curve between $x_i$ and $x_f$.

Example: for a spring with $F(x) = -kx$, compressing from $x = 0$ to $x = x_f$ gives \[ W = \int_0^{x_f} (-kx)\, dx = -\frac12 k x_f^2. \] The negative sign indicates the spring force removes kinetic energy (stores it as potential).

3. Work–Energy Theorem and Kinetic Energy

Consider a single particle moving along a line with net force $F_x$ and velocity $v$.

Start with Newton’s second law and velocity as $\frac{dx}{dt}$:

\[ F_x = m a_x = m \frac{dv}{dt}. \]

Multiply both sides by $v = \dfrac{dx}{dt}$:

\[ F_x v = m \frac{dv}{dt} v. \]

Note that $v\, dt = dx$, so

\[ F_x\, dx = m v\, dv. \]

Integrate from initial state (1) to final state (2):

\[ \int_{x_1}^{x_2} F_x\, dx = \int_{v_1}^{v_2} m v\, dv. \]

\[ W_{\text{net}} = \frac12 m v_2^2 - \frac12 m v_1^2 = \Delta K. \]

This is the work–energy theorem: \[ W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K. \] We define kinetic energy as \[ K = \frac12 m v^2. \]

4. Potential Energy and Conservative Forces

For a conservative force, we can define a potential energy function $U$ such that

\[ W_{\text{cons}} = -\Delta U. \]

Near Earth’s surface, with upward $+y$:

\[ U_g = m g y, \] and $\Delta U_g = mg (y_2 - y_1)$.

For an ideal spring (zero at $x=0$):

\[ U_s = \frac12 k x^2. \]

For a system with only conservative forces, \[ E_{\text{mech}} = K + U = \text{constant}. \]

5. Non-Conservative Forces and Energy Accounting

Non-conservative forces (like friction, many applied pushes, drag) do not have potential energies that can fully capture their effects. Instead, they contribute to energy changes directly:

\[ W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta K + \Delta U = \Delta E_{\text{mech}}. \]

Often, mechanical energy lost due to friction becomes thermal energy: \[ \Delta E_{\text{therm}} = -W_{\text{friction}}. \]

Engineering perspective: choose a system boundary, track all energy transfers in/out, and use energy conservation for the larger system (mechanical + thermal + other forms).

6. Power and Efficiency

Power is the rate of doing work or the rate of energy transfer.

\[ P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta W}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t}. \]

\[ P_{\text{inst}} = \frac{dW}{dt}. \]

If a constant force $\vec F$ acts on an object with instantaneous velocity $\vec v$:

\[ P = \vec F \cdot \vec v = F v \cos\theta. \]

Efficiency compares useful output power/energy to the input:

\[ \eta = \frac{P_{\text{out}}}{P_{\text{in}}} = \frac{E_{\text{out}}}{E_{\text{in}}}. \]

For machines or engines, efficiency is often quoted in percent. Humans converting chemical energy (food) to mechanical work also have efficiencies typically around 20–25%.

7. Summary of Key Relationships

  • Work (constant force): $W = \vec F \cdot \Delta \vec r = F \Delta r \cos\theta$.
  • Work (variable force in 1D): $W = \displaystyle\int_{x_i}^{x_f} F(x)\, dx$ (area under $F$–$x$).
  • Work–energy theorem: $W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K$ with $K = \frac12 m v^2$.
  • Gravitational potential near Earth: $U_g = m g y$; spring potential: $U_s = \frac12 k x^2$.
  • Conservative forces: $W_{\text{cons}} = -\Delta U$; mechanical energy $K+U$ is conserved if only conservative forces act.
  • Non-conservative forces: $W_{\text{nc}} = \Delta K + \Delta U$ account for changes in mechanical energy (often to thermal).
  • Power: $P = \dfrac{dW}{dt} = \vec F \cdot \vec v$; efficiency: $\eta = P_{\text{out}}/P_{\text{in}}$.

Example 1: Work by a Force at an Angle

A person pulls a 30 kg sled with a rope that makes a $30^\circ$ angle above the horizontal. The tension in the rope is $T = 80\ \text{N}$ and the sled is pulled 15 m along level ground.

  1. Find the work done by the tension force.
  2. Find the work done by the normal force and by gravity.
Show Solution

(1) Work by tension.

Displacement is horizontal, so angle between $\vec T$ and $\Delta \vec r$ is $30^\circ$.

\[ W_T = T \Delta r \cos\theta = (80)(15)\cos 30^\circ. \]

$\cos 30^\circ \approx 0.866$, so \[ W_T \approx 80 \cdot 15 \cdot 0.866 \approx 1039\ \text{J}. \]

(2) Work by normal force and gravity.

Both are perpendicular to the horizontal displacement.

\[ W_N = 0, \quad W_g = 0. \]

Example 2: Spring Work and Energy Conservation

A 0.50 kg block slides on a horizontal frictionless surface and compresses a spring with $k = 200\ \text{N/m}$ by 0.10 m before coming momentarily to rest.

  1. Find the work done by the spring on the block.
  2. Find the block’s initial speed before it touched the spring.
Show Solution

(1) Work done by spring.

For $F_s = -kx$, from $x=0$ to $x = x_f = 0.10\ \text{m}$: \[ W_s = \int_0^{x_f} (-kx)\, dx = -\frac12 k x_f^2. \]

\[ W_s = -\frac12 (200)(0.10)^2 = -\frac12 \cdot 200 \cdot 0.01 = -1.0\ \text{J}. \]

(2) Initial speed from energy.

Take the moment when the block just touches the spring as state 1 and the fully compressed, at rest position as state 2.

Initially, $K_1 = \frac12 m v_1^2$, $U_{s1} = 0$. Finally, $K_2 = 0$, $U_{s2} = \frac12 k x_f^2 = 1.0\ \text{J}$.

Energy conservation (no non-conservative forces): \[ K_1 + U_{s1} = K_2 + U_{s2} \Rightarrow \frac12 m v_1^2 = 1.0\ \text{J}. \]

\[ v_1 = \sqrt{\frac{2(1.0)}{0.50}} = \sqrt{4} = 2.0\ \text{m/s}. \]

Example 3: Car Braking with Friction (Energy Method)

A 1200 kg car traveling at 20 m/s skids to a stop on a level road. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the tires and road is $\mu_k = 0.80$.

  1. Use energy considerations to find the stopping distance.
  2. What average power is delivered to thermal energy if the car stops in 3.0 s?
Show Solution

(1) Stopping distance.

Initial kinetic energy: \[ K_i = \frac12 m v^2 = \frac12 (1200)(20^2) = 600 \cdot 400 = 2.4\times 10^5\ \text{J}. \]

Final kinetic energy $K_f = 0$. Friction does negative work: \[ W_{\text{fr}} = -f_k d = -\mu_k N\, d = -\mu_k m g\, d. \]

Energy equation: \[ K_i + W_{\text{fr}} = K_f \Rightarrow K_i - \mu_k m g\, d = 0. \]

Solve for $d$: \[ d = \frac{K_i}{\mu_k m g} = \frac{2.4\times 10^5}{0.80 \cdot 1200 \cdot 9.8}. \]

Denominator: $0.80 \cdot 1200 \cdot 9.8 \approx 0.80 \cdot 11760 \approx 9408$.

\[ d \approx \frac{2.4\times 10^5}{9.4\times 10^3} \approx 25.5\ \text{m}. \]

(2) Average power to thermal energy.

Energy converted to thermal: $\Delta E_{\text{therm}} = K_i = 2.4\times 10^5\ \text{J}$.

Over 3.0 s: \[ P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta E}{\Delta t} = \frac{2.4\times 10^5}{3.0} \approx 8.0\times 10^4\ \text{W} = 80\ \text{kW}. \]

Problem 1 – Variable Force Integral

A particle moves along the $x$-axis under a force $F(x) = 4x$ (in newtons) where $x$ is in meters. The particle moves from $x = 0$ to $x = 3.0\ \text{m}$.

  1. Compute the work done by the force using an integral.
  2. Would the work be positive or negative? Explain.

Solution (outline):

\[ W = \int_0^{3} 4x\, dx = 4 \left[ \frac12 x^2 \right]_0^{3} = 2 (9) = 18\ \text{J}. \]

Force and displacement are in the same direction for $x > 0$, so work is positive (speeding up or adding energy).

Problem 2 – Roller-Coaster Energy (No Friction)

A 500 kg roller-coaster car starts from rest at the top of a 40 m high hill. It then drops to a lower point 10 m above the ground. Neglect friction.

  1. Find its speed at the 10 m point using energy conservation.
  2. Verify the result with the work–energy theorem (using work by gravity).

Solution (outline):

Set $U = mgh$ with $h$ measured from ground. Initially at $h_i = 40$, finally at $h_f = 10$.

\[ K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f \Rightarrow 0 + mg(40) = \frac12 m v^2 + mg(10). \]

\[ mg(30) = \frac12 m v^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2 g \cdot 30} = \sqrt{60 g}. \] With $g \approx 9.8$, $v \approx \sqrt{588} \approx 24.3\ \text{m/s}$.

Work–energy theorem: $W_g = mg(h_i - h_f) = mg(30)$ equals $\Delta K = \frac12 m v^2$, same result.

Problem 3 – Lifting and Power

A small motor lifts a 50 kg crate vertically at constant speed to a height of 5.0 m in 8.0 s.

  1. Find the work done by the motor on the crate.
  2. Find the average power delivered by the motor.
  3. If the motor draws 900 W of electrical power, what is its efficiency?

Solution (outline):

Constant speed → net force zero; motor force equals weight, so work equals gain in gravitational potential energy: \[ W = mgh = 50 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 5.0 \approx 2450\ \text{J}. \]

Average power: \[ P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{W}{\Delta t} \approx \frac{2450}{8.0} \approx 306\ \text{W}. \]

Efficiency: \[ \eta = \frac{P_{\text{out}}}{P_{\text{in}}} = \frac{306}{900} \approx 0.34 \text{ or } 34\%. \]

Problem 4 – Energy Loss Due to Friction

A 2.0 kg block slides down a rough incline 4.0 m long at $30^\circ$ to the horizontal. Its speed at the top is 1.0 m/s and at the bottom is 5.0 m/s.

  1. Use energy to find the work done by friction.
  2. Comment on the sign of $W_{\text{fr}}$.

Solution (outline):

Height drop: $h = (4.0)\sin 30^\circ = 2.0\ \text{m}$.

\[ K_i = \frac12 (2.0)(1.0^2) = 1.0\ \text{J},\quad K_f = \frac12 (2.0)(5.0^2) = 25\ \text{J}. \]

\[ U_i = mg h = 2.0 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 2.0 \approx 39.2\ \text{J},\quad U_f = 0 \ (\text{take bottom as } y=0). \]

Energy accounting with friction: \[ K_i + U_i + W_{\text{fr}} = K_f + U_f. \]

\[ 1.0 + 39.2 + W_{\text{fr}} = 25,\quad W_{\text{fr}} = 25 - 40.2 = -15.2\ \text{J}. \]

Negative work: friction removed mechanical energy, converting it to thermal energy.
