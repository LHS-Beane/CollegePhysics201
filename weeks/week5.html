Week 5 • Momentum & Collisions

Linear Momentum, Impulse, and Collisions

This module develops linear momentum as a central tool in mechanics. You will relate impulse to the area under a force–time curve, apply conservation of momentum to explosions and collisions, and distinguish elastic, inelastic, and perfectly inelastic collisions in one and two dimensions.

Learning Goals

  • Define linear momentum \( \vec p = m\vec v \) and impulse \( \vec J \).
  • Connect impulse to the area under a force–time graph.
  • Identify when linear momentum is conserved for a system.
  • Apply conservation of momentum to 1D and 2D collisions and explosions.
  • Distinguish elastic, inelastic, and perfectly inelastic collisions and analyze energy changes.
  • Use component methods to handle 2D momentum problems conceptually and mathematically.

How to Use This Module

  • Read the Notes section to establish definitions and core relationships.
  • Draw quick sketches for every collision or explosion situation.
  • Work through the Worked Examples carefully; track momentum and energy.
  • Attempt the Practice Problems on your own, then compare with the solution outlines.
  • Use the Interactive Panel to experiment with 1D collisions numerically.

1. Linear Momentum and Impulse

The linear momentum of a particle (or rigid body treated as a particle) is \[ \vec p = m\vec v. \] It is a vector pointing in the direction of the velocity.

A net force acting over a time interval changes momentum. The impulse delivered by a net force \( \vec F_\text{net}(t) \) from time \(t_i\) to \(t_f\) is \[ \vec J = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} \vec F_\text{net}(t)\, dt, \] and the impulse–momentum theorem says \[ \vec J = \Delta \vec p = \vec p_f - \vec p_i. \]

For a constant net force \( \vec F_\text{net} \) over a time \( \Delta t \), \[ \vec J = \vec F_\text{net} \Delta t,\qquad \vec F_\text{net} = \frac{\Delta \vec p}{\Delta t}. \]

System momentum and external forces

For a system of particles, \[ \vec P_\text{sys} = \sum_i m_i \vec v_i. \] Internal forces between particles cancel in pairs (Newton’s 3rd law). Only external forces change the total system momentum: \[ \sum \vec F_\text{ext} = \frac{d\vec P_\text{sys}}{dt}. \]

2. Conservation of Linear Momentum (1D)

If the net external impulse on a system is zero over some time interval, the total momentum of the system is conserved: \[ \vec P_\text{initial} = \vec P_\text{final}. \]

In one dimension with objects 1 and 2, \[ m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}, \] where the sign of each velocity encodes direction.

Even if kinetic energy is not conserved (inelastic collision), total momentum of the isolated system is still conserved.

3. Elastic, Inelastic, and Perfectly Inelastic Collisions

In any collision between objects in an isolated system:

  • Momentum is always conserved: \( \vec P_i = \vec P_f \).
  • Kinetic energy may or may not be conserved.
Type Momentum? Kinetic Energy? Description
Elastic Conserved Conserved Idealized bouncy collision (no mechanical energy lost).
Inelastic Conserved Not conserved Some kinetic energy transforms into other forms (heat, sound, deformation).
Perfectly inelastic Conserved Not conserved Objects stick together and move with a common final velocity.

Perfectly inelastic collision in 1D

If masses \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) stick together, \[ m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2) v_f \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_f = \frac{m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i}}{m_1 + m_2}. \]

General 1D elastic collision

For two masses on a line, using both momentum and kinetic energy conservation, the final velocities can be written as \[ v_{1f} = \frac{m_1 - m_2}{m_1 + m_2} v_{1i} + \frac{2m_2}{m_1 + m_2} v_{2i}, \] \[ v_{2f} = \frac{2m_1}{m_1 + m_2} v_{1i} + \frac{m_2 - m_1}{m_1 + m_2} v_{2i}. \]

4. Momentum in Two Dimensions

In 2D collisions, momentum must be conserved in each component: \[ \sum p_{x,i} = \sum p_{x,f}, \qquad \sum p_{y,i} = \sum p_{y,f}. \]

Strategy:

  • Draw a vector diagram for the momenta before and after.
  • Write separate conservation equations for \(x\) and \(y\) components.
  • Use kinetic energy conservation only if the collision is elastic.

5. Center of Mass and Recoil (Conceptual)

For a system of particles, the center-of-mass position is \[ \vec r_\text{CM} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_i m_i \vec r_i,\quad M = \sum_i m_i. \] The total momentum is related to the center-of-mass motion by \[ \vec P_\text{sys} = M \vec v_\text{CM}. \]

If there is no net external force on the system, then \(\vec P_\text{sys}\) is constant and the center of mass moves with constant velocity, even if the objects inside the system interact strongly (explosions, recoil, internal springs, etc.).

Example: when a rifle recoils as a bullet is fired, the center of mass of the rifle–bullet system continues its motion smoothly; the forward momentum of the bullet is balanced by the backward momentum of the rifle so that the total remains constant.

Example 1: Rear-End Collision on a Frictionless Track (1D)

A 1,000 kg car moving at \(20\,\text{m/s}\) to the right collides with a 1,500 kg SUV moving at \(10\,\text{m/s}\) in the same direction. The collision is perfectly inelastic, and the vehicles lock together and move as one afterward.

  1. Find the common final velocity of the two-vehicle system.
  2. Determine the percentage of kinetic energy lost in the collision.
Show Solution

(1) Final velocity using momentum conservation:

Let right be positive. Before: \[ p_i = m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (1000)(20) + (1500)(10) = 20{,}000 + 15{,}000 = 35{,}000\,\text{kg·m/s}. \]

After, they stick: mass \(m_1 + m_2 = 2500\,\text{kg}\) with velocity \(v_f\): \[ p_f = (m_1 + m_2) v_f. \] Momentum conservation \(p_i = p_f\) gives \[ v_f = \frac{35{,}000}{2500} = 14\,\text{m/s} \text{ (to the right)}. \]

(2) Kinetic energy change:

Initial kinetic energy: \[ K_i = \tfrac12 m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \tfrac12 m_2 v_{2i}^2 = \tfrac12 (1000)(20^2) + \tfrac12 (1500)(10^2). \]

\[ K_i = 500 \cdot 400 + 750 \cdot 100 = 200{,}000 + 75{,}000 = 275{,}000\,\text{J}. \]

Final kinetic energy: \[ K_f = \tfrac12 (2500) (14^2) = 1250 \cdot 196 = 245{,}000\,\text{J}. \]

Energy lost: \[ \Delta K = K_f - K_i = 245{,}000 - 275{,}000 = -30{,}000\,\text{J}. \]

Percentage loss: \[ \frac{|\Delta K|}{K_i} \times 100\% = \frac{30{,}000}{275{,}000} \times 100\% \approx 10.9\%. \]

Final speed \(v_f = 14\,\text{m/s}\) to the right; about \(11\%\) of the kinetic energy was lost.

Example 2: 2D Collision on Ice

Two identical 80 kg hockey players collide on a frictionless rink. Player A is initially moving east at \(4.0\,\text{m/s}\). Player B is initially at rest. After they collide and hook together, they move off at an angle of \(30^\circ\) north of east.

  1. Find their common final speed.
  2. Determine how much kinetic energy is lost in the collision.
Show Solution

(1) Final speed using momentum conservation:

Let east be +\(x\), north be +\(y\). Initial momentum: \[ \vec P_i = (m v, 0) = (80 \cdot 4.0, 0) = (320, 0)\,\text{kg·m/s}. \] Player B is at rest.

After, combined mass is \(M = 160\,\text{kg}\) with speed \(v_f\) at \(30^\circ\) above east: \[ \vec P_f = M v_f (\cos 30^\circ, \sin 30^\circ) = 160 v_f (\cos 30^\circ, \tfrac12). \]

Momentum conservation in \(x\): \[ 320 = 160 v_f \cos 30^\circ \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_f = \frac{320}{160 \cos 30^\circ} = \frac{2}{\cos 30^\circ}. \]

\(\cos 30^\circ = \sqrt{3}/2 \approx 0.866\), so \[ v_f \approx \frac{2}{0.866} \approx 2.31\,\text{m/s}. \]

(2) Kinetic energy loss:

Initial kinetic energy (only player A moving): \[ K_i = \tfrac12 (80) (4.0^2) = 40 \cdot 16 = 640\,\text{J}. \]

Final kinetic energy: \[ K_f = \tfrac12 (160) (2.31^2) \approx 80 \cdot 5.34 \approx 427\,\text{J}. \]

Energy lost: \[ \Delta K = K_f - K_i \approx 427 - 640 = -213\,\text{J}. \]

Percentage loss: \[ \frac{213}{640} \times 100\% \approx 33\%. \]

Final speed \(v_f \approx 2.3\,\text{m/s}\); roughly one-third of the kinetic energy was lost.

Practice Set – Week 5

Try each problem fully before expanding the solution outline. Focus on clearly defining the system, setting up conservation equations, and tracking energy changes when appropriate.

Problem 1 – Impulse from a Force–Time Graph

A 0.15 kg hockey puck initially at rest is struck by a stick. During a 10 ms contact interval, the force on the puck rises linearly from 0 N to 120 N, then drops linearly back to 0 N. Treat the force–time graph as a symmetric triangle.

  1. Find the impulse delivered to the puck.
  2. Determine the puck’s speed immediately after the hit.

Solution (outline):

Impulse is the area under the \(F(t)\) graph, which is a triangle: \[ J = \tfrac12 (\text{base}) (\text{height}) = \tfrac12 (0.010\,\text{s})(120\,\text{N}) = 0.60\,\text{N·s}. \]

Use \(J = \Delta p = m v_f\) (initial momentum zero): \[ v_f = \frac{J}{m} = \frac{0.60}{0.15} = 4.0\,\text{m/s}. \]

Problem 2 – Perfectly Inelastic Collision

A 2.0 kg cart moving at \(3.0\,\text{m/s}\) to the right collides with a 1.0 kg cart moving at \(1.0\,\text{m/s}\) to the left on a frictionless track. The carts stick together.

  1. Find the final velocity (magnitude and direction) of the combined carts.
  2. Compute the percentage of kinetic energy lost in the collision.

Solution (outline):

Take right as positive. Then \(v_{1i} = +3.0\,\text{m/s}\), \(v_{2i} = -1.0\,\text{m/s}\). Total initial momentum: \[ p_i = (2.0)(3.0) + (1.0)(-1.0) = 6.0 - 1.0 = 5.0\,\text{kg·m/s}. \]

Total mass after sticking: \(3.0\,\text{kg}\). \[ v_f = \frac{p_i}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{5.0}{3.0} \approx 1.67\,\text{m/s} \text{ to the right}. \]

Initial kinetic energy: \[ K_i = \tfrac12(2.0)(3.0^2) + \tfrac12(1.0)(1.0^2) = 1.0 \cdot 9 + 0.5 = 9.5\,\text{J}. \] Final kinetic energy: \[ K_f = \tfrac12 (3.0)(1.67^2) \approx 1.5 \cdot 2.78 \approx 4.17\,\text{J}. \]

Energy lost \( \approx 9.5 - 4.2 = 5.3\,\text{J} \), percentage loss \[ \approx \frac{5.3}{9.5} \times 100\% \approx 56\%. \]

Problem 3 – 2D Momentum After Collision

A 0.20 kg ball moving at \(4.0\,\text{m/s}\) east collides with a 0.30 kg ball that is initially at rest. After the collision, the 0.20 kg ball moves at \(2.0\,\text{m/s}\) at \(40^\circ\) north of east.

  1. Find the speed and direction of the 0.30 kg ball after the collision.
  2. Is this collision elastic? Justify briefly using kinetic energy.

Solution (outline):

Let east be +\(x\), north be +\(y\). Initial momentum: \[ \vec P_i = (0.20)(4.0, 0) = (0.80, 0)\,\text{kg·m/s}. \]

After, 0.20 kg ball: \[ \vec p_1 = (0.20)(2.0)(\cos 40^\circ, \sin 40^\circ) = 0.40(\cos 40^\circ, \sin 40^\circ). \]

Let the 0.30 kg ball have momentum \( \vec p_2 = (p_{2x}, p_{2y}) \). Then \[ \vec p_2 = \vec P_i - \vec p_1. \] Divide by 0.30 kg to find its velocity components, then magnitude and angle.

Compare \(K_i = \tfrac12 (0.20)(4.0^2)\) with \(K_f = \tfrac12(0.20)(2.0^2) + \tfrac12(0.30)v_2^2\). If \(K_f < K_i\), the collision is inelastic.
