Week 5 • Momentum & Collisions

Linear Momentum, Impulse, and Collisions

This week develops the impulse–momentum theorem, conservation of momentum in one and two dimensions, and energy analysis of collisions — perfectly inelastic, elastic, and explosion-type scenarios.

  • • Define and compute linear momentum and impulse.
  • • Apply conservation of momentum to multi-object systems.
  • • Distinguish elastic vs. inelastic collisions using energy.
  • • Work with 2D collision geometry and vector components.

Week 5 Overview & Learning Goals

Momentum provides a powerful alternative to force and energy methods, especially for interacting multi-object systems and collisions. You’ll use both calculus and algebraic arguments to connect force, impulse, and changes in momentum.

  • Define linear momentum $\vec p = m\vec v$ and impulse $\vec J = \int \vec F\,dt$.
  • Relate net impulse to change in momentum: $\vec J = \Delta \vec p$.
  • Identify when total momentum of a system is conserved.
  • Analyze 1D collisions (elastic, inelastic, and completely inelastic).
  • Resolve momentum conservation into components for 2D collisions.
  • Compare energy and momentum methods; identify where kinetic energy is lost.

Key Concepts & Equations

Momentum & Impulse

  • Linear momentum: $\\vec p = m\\vec v$.
  • Impulse: $\\vec J = \\int \\vec F\\,dt$.
  • Impulse–momentum theorem: $\\vec J = \\Delta \\vec p$.
  • Average force over time interval $\\Delta t$: $F_\\text{avg} = \\dfrac{\\Delta p}{\\Delta t}$.

System & Conservation

  • Isolated system: external impulse ≈ 0, so total momentum conserved.
  • $\\sum \\vec p_i = \\sum \\vec p_f$ (vector equation).
  • Internal forces cancel in pairs (Newton’s 3rd law).

Collisions in 1D

  • Perfectly inelastic: objects stick → one final velocity $v_f$. $$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2)v_f.$$
  • Elastic: momentum and kinetic energy both conserved.
  • Useful relation in 1D elastic collision: relative speed is reversed: $$v_{1i} - v_{2i} = -(v_{1f} - v_{2f}).$$

2D Collisions

  • Conserve $p_x$ and $p_y$ separately: $$\\sum p_{x,i} = \\sum p_{x,f}, \\quad \\sum p_{y,i} = \\sum p_{y,f}.$$
  • Use components for unknown angles or speeds.

Worked Examples (Overview)

Full step-by-step solutions are in the Week 5 Examples PDF . The in-class examples typically include:

  1. Impulse from a force–time graph.
    Given a triangular force pulse on a cart, find the change in momentum and final velocity.
  2. Perfectly inelastic collision in 1D.
    Two carts stick together; solve for final speed and energy lost.
  3. Elastic collision in 1D.
    Use both momentum and kinetic energy to solve for both final velocities.
  4. 2D collision with angles.
    A moving puck strikes another at rest; after collision, one angle is known. Use component equations to solve for the other angle or speed.
  5. Explosion / recoil problem.
    A stationary object breaks into two pieces; find recoil speed or fragment velocities.

Reflection & Extension

  • Compare how you would solve a collision using: (a) Newton’s 2nd law directly, (b) work–energy, (c) momentum conservation. When is each most efficient?
  • Think about real-world systems (car crashes, rockets, sports). Where is momentum an easier tool than forces or energy?
  • Preview connection to rotation: angular momentum plays a similar role for rotating systems.
