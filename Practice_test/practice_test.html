Question 1

A bowling ball rolls over a smooth horizontal floor with constant velocity. The measured position–time data are shown below.

Four students (Antonio, Bela, Charles, and Dominique) draw different straight red lines on the graph (shown below) and calculate the slope of their line as the velocity.

Which student's analysis will result in the most accurate calculation of the ball's velocity during the six-second time interval shown?

  • A) Dominique
  • B) Bela
  • C) Antonio
  • D) Charles

Answer (likely): D – Charles (verify with your official key).

To get the best estimate of the constant velocity from noisy experimental data, you draw a best-fit line that represents the average trend of all the points over the entire interval. The student whose red line passes closest to most points and spans the full time (rather than just connecting two arbitrary points or using only a subset) will produce the most accurate velocity.

Question 3

In the absence of air resistance, an object dropped near the surface of Earth moves with a constant downward acceleration of 9.8 m/s². This means that:

  • A) The object falls 9.8 m during each second.
  • B) The speed of the object increases by 9.8 m/s during each second.
  • C) The speed of the object increases by 9.8 m/s during the first second only.
  • D) The speed of the object as it falls is 9.8 m/s.
  • E) The object falls 9.8 m during the first second only.

Answer: B

Constant acceleration means the change in velocity per unit time is constant. Here, \(a = \Delta v / \Delta t = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2\), so each second the speed increases by 9.8 m/s. The distance fallen each second does not stay constant; it increases as the object speeds up.

Question 4

An object is shot vertically upward with a positive initial velocity. Taking upward as positive, which of the following correctly describes the velocity and acceleration at the object’s maximum height?

  • A) Velocity: negative; Acceleration: negative
  • B) Velocity: positive; Acceleration: negative
  • C) Velocity: zero; Acceleration: zero
  • D) Velocity: zero; Acceleration: negative
  • E) Velocity: positive; Acceleration: positive

Answer: D

At the top of its trajectory, the object is instantaneously at rest, so \(v = 0\). However, gravity still acts downward with magnitude \(g = 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2\), so the acceleration is negative in the chosen coordinate system (up positive).

Question 8

Question 10

An object attached to an ideal spring (obeying Hooke’s law) undergoes SHM after release. You wish to double the total mechanical energy of the object–spring–Earth system. Which of the following changes would accomplish this?

  • A) Double both the amplitude and the spring constant.
  • B) Double the mass of the object.
  • C) Double both the mass and the amplitude.
  • D) Double the spring constant.
  • E) Double the amplitude of vibration.

Answer: D

For a spring–mass system, the total energy is \(E = \tfrac12 k A^2\). To double \(E\) while holding amplitude fixed, you must double \(k\): then \(E' = \tfrac12 (2k)A^2 = 2E\). Doubling amplitude would give \(E' = \tfrac12 k (2A)^2 = 4E\), i.e., four times the energy. The mass does not appear in the expression for total energy (given fixed amplitude).

Question 11

Two simple pendulums, A and B, each have length 3.0 m. Pendulum A has mass twice that of B. The period of pendulum A is \(T\). What is the period of pendulum B?

  • A) \(T/2\)
  • B) \(T\sqrt{2}\)
  • C) \(T/\sqrt{2}\)
  • D) \(T\)
  • E) \(2T\)

Answer: D

For a small-angle simple pendulum, \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\), which depends only on the length and gravitational acceleration, not on the mass. Since both pendulums have the same length, they have the same period, \(T_B = T\).

Question 12

An object is attached to a vertical spring and oscillates between two turning points A and B. At what location is its kinetic energy maximum?

  • A) One-fourth of the way between A and B
  • B) At either A or B
  • C) One-third of the way between A and B
  • D) Midway between A and B
  • E) At none of the above points

Answer: D

In SHM, kinetic energy is maximal when the speed is maximal. For a spring–mass system, the speed is greatest as the mass passes through the equilibrium position, which is halfway between the turning points A and B. At A and B, the speed (and hence kinetic energy) is zero.

Question 13

A ball swings at the end of a massless string, undergoing circular motion as shown. At which point(s) is the magnitude of the radial (centripetal) acceleration greatest?

  • A) A and D
  • B) B
  • C) A and C
  • D) A and B
  • E) C

Answer: B

The radial acceleration is \(a_r = v^2 / r\). For a pendulum, the speed is greatest at the bottom of the swing (point B), where all the gravitational potential energy lost from the top has become kinetic energy. Since \(r\) is fixed and \(v\) is largest at B, the radial acceleration is largest at B.

Question 14

A wheel is rotating with a constant angular acceleration \(\alpha\). Which of the following must be true?

  • A) The net torque on the wheel is constant.
  • B) The magnitude of the angular velocity is constant.
  • C) The tangential acceleration vector of a point at the rim is constant (vector).
  • D) The speed of a point at the rim is constant.
  • E) The net force on the wheel is constant.

Answer: A

For a rigid body of fixed moment of inertia \(I\), the rotational analog of Newton’s second law is \(\tau_\text{net} = I\alpha\). If \(\alpha\) is constant and \(I\) is constant, then the net torque must be constant. The angular velocity cannot be constant if \(\alpha \neq 0\), and the tangential acceleration vector changes direction as the wheel rotates, so it is not a constant vector.

Question 15

A thin metal hoop, a thick wooden hoop, and a solid plastic disk all have the same mass and outer radius. A string is wrapped around each and pulled with the same constant force \(F\), causing them to rotate.

Rank the resulting angular accelerations \(\alpha_A\), \(\alpha_B\), and \(\alpha_C\) of the three objects from least to greatest.

  • A) \(\alpha_A = \alpha_B < \alpha_C\)
  • B) \(\alpha_A = \alpha_B = \alpha_C\)
  • C) \(\alpha_A < \alpha_B < \alpha_C\)
  • D) \(\alpha_A < \alpha_C < \alpha_B\)
  • E) \(\alpha_C < \alpha_B < \alpha_A\)

Answer: C

For the same torque \(\tau = F R\), the angular acceleration satisfies \(\alpha = \tau / I\). The moment of inertia is largest for the thin hoop (mass concentrated at the rim), smaller for the thick hoop, and smallest for the solid disk. So \[ I_A > I_B > I_C \quad \Rightarrow \quad \alpha_A < \alpha_B < \alpha_C. \]

Question 16

An iron hoop and a wooden disk of equal mass and radius roll without slipping on level ground with the same center-of-mass speed. Each then rolls up a frictionless incline (with possibly different angles) and comes to rest.

The hoop rises to a vertical height \(h_1\) and the disk to \(h_2\) above their starting height. How do \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) compare?

  • A) \(h_1 = h_2\)
  • B) \(h_1 < h_2\)
  • C) \(h_1 > h_2\)
  • D) It depends on the angles of the inclines.

Answer: C

Both objects have translational and rotational kinetic energy at the bottom. For a given center-of-mass speed, the hoop (with \(I = mR^2\)) has more rotational kinetic energy than the disk (\(I = \tfrac12 mR^2\)). So the hoop starts with a larger total mechanical energy and will climb to a greater height: \(h = E/(mg)\) is largest for the hoop.

Question 17

Identical blocks are attached to identical platforms in two different orientations (I and II), each able to rotate about the same central vertical axis.

Which statement correctly compares the rotational inertia of the apparatus in the two orientations?

  • A) The rotational inertia is greater in orientation I.
  • B) The rotational inertia is the same in both orientations.
  • C) The rotational inertia is greater in orientation II.
  • D) There is not enough information to compare them.

Answer (typical): C

Rotational inertia \(I = \sum m r^2\) increases as mass is moved farther from the axis of rotation. In the usual version of this problem, the blocks in orientation II are farther from the central axis than in orientation I, so orientation II has the greater rotational inertia.

Question 18

Two identical uniform disks can spin freely on frictionless axles. Initially, the left disk rotates at a constant angular speed and the right disk is at rest.

The left disk is slowly pressed into contact with the right disk until they rotate together at the same angular speed.

Compare the initial total rotational momentum of the two-disk system to the final total rotational momentum.

  • A) The initial total rotational momentum is less than the final.
  • B) The initial total rotational momentum is greater than the final.
  • C) The relationship depends on the coefficient of friction between the disks.
  • D) The initial and final total rotational momentum are equal.

Answer: D

No external torques act on the two-disk system about the rotation axis. Internal friction torques between the disks can redistribute angular momentum but cannot change the total angular momentum. Therefore, the total initial rotational momentum equals the total final rotational momentum.

Question 19

Three objects are thrown from the roof of a building with the same initial speed. Object I is thrown horizontally. Object II is thrown at 30° above the horizontal. Object III is thrown at 30° below the horizontal. Air resistance is negligible.

Just before they hit the ground, how do their speeds \(v_{If}\), \(v_{IIf}\), and \(v_{IIIf}\) compare?

  • A) \(v_{If} < v_{IIf} < v_{IIIf}\)
  • B) \(v_{If} < v_{IIf} = v_{IIIf}\)
  • C) \(v_{IIIf} < v_{IIf} < v_{If}\)
  • D) \(v_{If} = v_{IIf} = v_{IIIf}\)
  • E) \(v_{IIf} = v_{IIIf} < v_{If}\)

Answer: D

With no air resistance, total mechanical energy is conserved. Each object starts at the same height with the same initial speed, so each has the same initial total energy \(E_i = \tfrac12 m v_0^2 + mg h\). Just before hitting the ground, they all have the same potential energy (same final height), so the same final kinetic energy \(\tfrac12 m v_f^2\). Therefore, \(v_{If} = v_{IIf} = v_{IIIf}\).

Question 20

You throw a baseball straight up and consider the system consisting of just the baseball. Compare the sign of the work done by Earth’s gravitational force while the ball moves up and while it moves down.

  • A) Positive on the way up; positive on the way down.
  • B) Negative on the way up; negative on the way down.
  • C) Positive on the way up; negative on the way down.
  • D) Zero on both parts of the motion.
  • E) Negative on the way up; positive on the way down.

Answer: E

The gravitational force always points downward. On the way up, the displacement is upward, so the angle between force and displacement is 180°, and the work \(W = \vec{F}\cdot\vec{d}\) is negative. On the way down, the displacement is downward, parallel to the force, so the work is positive.

Question 21

A truck has four times the mass of a car and moves with twice the car’s speed. If \(K_t\) and \(K_c\) are the kinetic energies of the truck and car, respectively, which is true?

  • A) \(K_t = 2 K_c\)
  • B) \(K_t = K_c\)
  • C) \(K_t = 16 K_c\)
  • D) \(K_t = \tfrac12 K_c\)
  • E) \(K_t = 4 K_c\)

Answer: C

Kinetic energy is \(K = \tfrac12 m v^2\). Let the car have mass \(m\) and speed \(v\), so \(K_c = \tfrac12 m v^2\). The truck then has mass \(4m\) and speed \(2v\): \[ K_t = \tfrac12 (4m)(2v)^2 = \tfrac12 (4m)(4v^2) = 8mv^2 = 16\left(\tfrac12 m v^2\right) = 16 K_c. \]

Question 24

At a water park, swimmers can choose between two frictionless slides that start at the same height \(h\). Slide 1 is straight; Slide 2 is curved, dropping steeply at first then leveling out.

Let \(v_1\) be the speed at the bottom of slide 1 and \(v_2\) the speed at the end of slide 2. How do they compare?

  • A) \(v_1 < v_2\)
  • B) \(v_1 = v_2\)
  • C) \(v_1 > v_2\)
  • D) The heavier swimmer is always faster, regardless of slide.
  • E) More information about the shape of slide 2 is needed.

Answer: B

With no friction, mechanical energy is conserved. Both swimmers start at the same height, so they lose the same amount of gravitational potential energy \(mgh\), which becomes kinetic energy \(\tfrac12 m v^2\) at the bottom. Thus \(v = \sqrt{2gh}\) is the same for any path between the same initial and final heights.

Question 25

A cart of mass \(m\) is initially at rest on a horizontal, frictionless track. A horizontal force is applied, and its value vs. time is shown below.

Which statement about the cart’s momentum during the 0–8 s interval is true?

  • A) Its momentum is zero at \(t = 4\,\text{s}\).
  • B) Its initial momentum is opposite in direction to its final momentum.
  • C) Its initial momentum is equal to its final momentum.
  • D) Its initial momentum is positive and its final momentum is negative.

Answer (typical): C — if the positive and negative areas under the force–time curve cancel.

The change in momentum is the impulse: \(\Delta p = \int F\,dt\), which is the signed area under the \(F(t)\) curve. If the graph’s positive area equals its negative area over 0–8 s, then the net impulse is zero and the final momentum equals the initial momentum (both zero). Check the graph to see whether the positive and negative regions balance.

Question 26

During a head-on collision, how does an air bag protect a passenger in the car?

  • A) It decreases the initial momentum of the passenger.
  • B) It shortens the stopping time and increases the force on the passenger.
  • C) It lengthens the stopping time and decreases the force on the passenger.
  • D) It decreases the impulse exerted on the passenger.

Answer: C

The passenger’s momentum must change from its initial value to near zero in the crash; the impulse \(\Delta p\) is essentially fixed by the change in velocity. Since \(\Delta p = F_\text{avg}\,\Delta t\), increasing the stopping time \(\Delta t\) reduces the average force \(F_\text{avg}\). Air bags accomplish this by allowing the passenger to come to rest over a longer distance and time, reducing peak forces.

Question 27

A small car collides head-on with a large truck and they stick together. Which statement about the magnitude of the momentum change for each vehicle is correct? (There may be more than one correct choice.)

  • A) The magnitude of the momentum change of each is inversely proportional to its mass.
  • B) The truck experiences the greater magnitude momentum change.
  • C) The magnitude of the momentum change of each is directly proportional to its mass.
  • D) The car and truck experience the same magnitude momentum change.
  • E) The small car experiences the greater magnitude momentum change.

Answer: D

In an isolated two-object collision, the internal forces between the objects are equal and opposite. The impulses on each object are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction, so the magnitude of the momentum change \(|\Delta p|\) is the same for both. The car typically experiences a much larger acceleration because it has the smaller mass, but the magnitude of momentum change is equal.
